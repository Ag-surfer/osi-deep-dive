## Where BGP sits

BGP is the **exterior gateway protocol** that stitches the internet's ~75,000 **autonomous systems**
(AS) into one reachable whole. An IGP like [OSPF](../ospf/) finds the *shortest* path inside one
administrative domain; BGP finds a **policy-compliant** path *between* domains — where "shortest" is
meaningless because the hops belong to competing companies with contracts, not a shared metric. It is a
[**path-vector**](../routing-concepts/) protocol: it advertises reachable prefixes tagged with the full
**AS_PATH** they traversed, and it chooses routes by a deterministic pipeline of **attributes**, not by
a distance calculation.

<Callout variant="insight" title="BGP's defining trait: policy over distance">
Every other protocol on these pages optimizes a number (hop count, cost, composite metric). BGP
optimizes **business policy**. Local Preference says "prefer my cheap transit provider over my
expensive one" even if the expensive one is topologically closer. The AS_PATH provides loop prevention
(reject any path containing your own AS) and a coarse length metric — but it is *far* down the decision
list, below several policy knobs. Internalize that and BGP stops being mysterious.
</Callout>

## Autonomous systems, eBGP, and iBGP

An **AS** is a network under one administrative/routing policy, identified by a **32-bit AS number**
(ASN; the original 16-bit space, 0–65535, was exhausted). BGP runs in two modes that behave very
differently:

- **eBGP** — between routers in *different* ASes. Peers are usually directly connected (TTL 1), the
  advertising router rewrites **NEXT_HOP** to itself, and the AS prepends its ASN to the AS_PATH.
- **iBGP** — between routers in the *same* AS. Peers can be any distance apart (the session rides the
  IGP), the AS_PATH and NEXT_HOP are **not** changed, and crucially:

<Callout variant="warning" title="The iBGP split-horizon rule → the full-mesh problem">
A router **must not** re-advertise an iBGP-learned route to another iBGP peer (there's no AS_PATH change
inside the AS, so there'd be no loop protection). The consequence: every iBGP speaker must peer with
**every other** iBGP speaker — a full mesh of `n(n−1)/2` sessions. At 50 routers that's 1,225 sessions.
**Route reflectors** and **confederations** (below) exist solely to break this `n²` explosion.
</Callout>

## The BGP session

BGP runs over **TCP port 179** — it inherits TCP's reliability, ordering, and flow control rather than
reinventing them. A session walks a finite state machine and, once **Established**, exchanges routes
incrementally (only changes thereafter), kept alive by KEEPALIVEs within the negotiated **hold time**.

BGP uses four message types: **OPEN** (negotiate ASN/hold-time/capabilities), **UPDATE** (advertise NLRI
with attributes, and withdraw routes), **KEEPALIVE** (liveness), and **NOTIFICATION** (signal an error
and tear the session down).

## Path attributes

Routes carry **path attributes** — the inputs to every policy and the best-path decision. They fall
into four classes by how peers must handle unknown ones:

| Attribute | Class | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **ORIGIN** | Well-known mandatory | How the route entered BGP: IGP (`i`) < EGP (`e`) < Incomplete (`?`) |
| **AS_PATH** | Well-known mandatory | Ordered list of ASes traversed; loop check + length metric |
| **NEXT_HOP** | Well-known mandatory | The IP to forward toward (recursively resolved via the IGP) |
| **LOCAL_PREF** | Well-known discretionary | AS-wide outbound preference (higher wins); never leaves the AS |
| **MED** | Optional non-transitive | "Multi-Exit Discriminator" hint to a neighbor AS (lower wins) |
| **COMMUNITY** | Optional transitive | A taggable label for policy (e.g. `NO_EXPORT`) |
| **ORIGINATOR_ID / CLUSTER_LIST** | Optional non-transitive | Route-reflector loop prevention |

<Callout variant="note" title="WEIGHT is Cisco-only and never advertised">
**Weight** is a Cisco-proprietary, router-*local* selector — it isn't a BGP attribute and is never sent
to any peer. It's the very first tiebreak, so it's the bluntest "I prefer this path on this box" hammer.
Don't confuse it with Local Preference, which *is* a real attribute carried AS-wide in iBGP.
</Callout>

## The best-path decision process

When BGP holds several routes to the same prefix, it runs a strict, ordered elimination — the single
most important thing to know cold at CCNP/expert level. Step through it on a real candidate set:

The full order: **Weight** (Cisco-local) → **Local Preference** → **locally originated** → **AS-Path
length** → **Origin** (i\<e\<?) → **MED** → **eBGP over iBGP** → **lowest IGP metric to NEXT_HOP** →
(oldest eBGP / maximal-paths for ECMP) → **lowest Router ID** → lowest neighbor address.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Two traps in the decision process">
- **MED is only compared between routes from the *same* neighboring AS** (by default). Routes from
  different neighbor ASes skip the MED comparison entirely — a frequent source of "why didn't my MED
  win?" confusion. `bgp always-compare-med` overrides this, with care.
- **The decision is per-prefix and deterministic**, but **Weight and Local Preference sit above AS-Path
  length** — so a longer AS path routinely wins. Operators shape traffic with those knobs precisely
  because they outrank the "natural" path length.
</Callout>

## NEXT_HOP and the recursive lookup

Across an eBGP boundary the advertising router sets **NEXT_HOP** to itself. Within the AS, iBGP leaves
NEXT_HOP **unchanged** — so an iBGP route's next hop is some *external* address the receiving router may
have no direct route to. Two fixes: the IGP carries reachability to that next hop (BGP does a
**recursive lookup**: prefix → BGP next-hop → IGP route → real interface), or the border router applies
**next-hop-self** so iBGP peers forward to *it*. A "route is in the table but not used" symptom is very
often an **unreachable or invalid NEXT_HOP**.

## Scaling iBGP: route reflectors and confederations

To escape the full mesh:

- **Route reflectors (RR)** relax the iBGP split-horizon rule: an RR may *reflect* routes between its
  **clients**, so clients peer only with the RR(s). Loops are prevented by two new attributes —
  **ORIGINATOR_ID** (the route's origin inside the AS) and **CLUSTER_LIST** (the RR clusters it passed
  through); a router drops a route whose list already contains its own cluster.
- **Confederations** split one AS into smaller **sub-ASes** that run eBGP-like sessions between
  themselves (intra-confederation), while appearing externally as a single ASN.

## Communities and policy

A **community** is an arbitrary tag attached to a route so policy can act on groups of prefixes without
listing them. **Well-known** communities trigger standard behavior — **NO_EXPORT** (don't advertise
beyond the AS), **NO_ADVERTISE** (don't advertise to *any* peer), **NO_EXPORT_SUBCONFED**. Providers
publish community schemes so customers can signal intent ("prepend twice in region X", "set local-pref
80", "blackhole this /32 under attack"). **Large communities** (<RFCRef n={8092} />) restored room for
32-bit ASNs that standard 32-bit communities couldn't express.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Inbound vs. outbound traffic engineering — an asymmetry">
You fully control your **outbound** path selection with **Local Preference** and **Weight**. You only
*influence* your **inbound** traffic, because remote ASes make their own choices: **AS-Path prepending**
(make a path look longer), **MED** (only to a directly-connected AS, only sometimes honored), and
**communities** the upstream agrees to act on. Inbound TE is a negotiation, not a setting — the deepest
practical truth in BGP operations.
</Callout>

## Convergence and stability (expert)

BGP is **deliberately slow** — global stability outranks fast reaction. Key mechanisms:

- **MRAI (Minimum Route Advertisement Interval)** — rate-limits UPDATEs per prefix per peer (≈30 s eBGP
  default), damping churn at the cost of convergence time.
- **Path hunting** — on withdrawal, a router may try successively longer alternate AS paths before
  declaring a prefix dead, briefly exploring invalid routes. A fundamental path-vector behavior.
- **Route-flap damping** (<RFCRef n={7454} />) — penalize and temporarily suppress prefixes that flap;
  use carefully (it can over-suppress legitimate changes).
- **BFD for BGP** — sub-second peer-failure detection without aggressive hold timers.
- **Add-Path** — advertise *multiple* paths per prefix so RR clients can see backups and do ECMP, which
  best-path's single-winner default otherwise hides.

## Security considerations

<Callout variant="security" title="BGP trusts what it's told — that's the whole problem">
- **Prefix hijacking** — announcing a prefix you don't own (accidentally or maliciously) draws its
  traffic to you. **RPKI + Route Origin Validation (ROV)** (<RFCRef n={6811} />) cryptographically binds
  a prefix to its authorized origin ASN, so peers can drop **invalid** announcements.
- **Route leaks** — violating the **valley-free / Gao-Rexford** rules (e.g. re-advertising a provider's
  routes to another provider) turns a stub into unwanted transit. Defended with strict prefix/AS-path
  filters and the **"down-only"** community signaling (RFC 9234 roles).
- **AS_PATH manipulation** — forging a shorter path; **BGPsec** signs the path, but deployment is thin —
  origin validation (RPKI) is the realistic defense today.
- **Session attacks** — protect TCP 179 with **TCP-AO/MD5**, **GTSM/TTL-security** (eBGP is TTL 1, so
  reject anything farther), and **`maximum-prefix`** limits so a misbehaving peer can't flood your RIB.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms and the cause behind them">
- **Stuck in Active/Connect** — the TCP 179 session can't establish: ACL blocking 179, no IGP route to
  the peer, wrong neighbor address, or `update-source` mismatch on a loopback-based iBGP session.
- **Established but no routes / not propagating** — missing **iBGP full mesh or RR**; or a route filtered
  by an import/export policy; or **next-hop-self** not set so the NEXT_HOP is unreachable.
- **Route present but not selected** — an **invalid/unreachable NEXT_HOP**, or it simply lost the
  best-path decision (walk the steps above).
- **Constant flapping** — an unstable link/peer; stabilize it, consider damping, and use BFD for clean
  detection.
- **`maximum-prefix` shutdown** — a peer sent more prefixes than your limit (often a leak); investigate
  before raising the cap.
</Callout>
