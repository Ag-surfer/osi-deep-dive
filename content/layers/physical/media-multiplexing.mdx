## The medium and its impairments

Every signal fights the same four enemies as it crosses a medium, and each medium trades them
differently:

- **Attenuation** — the signal weakens with distance, setting the maximum reach before a repeater.
- **Noise** — thermal, electromagnetic, crosstalk from neighbors; it sets the SNR and thus the
  [Shannon capacity](../modulation-capacity/).
- **Distortion / dispersion** — different frequencies travel at slightly different speeds, smearing
  symbols into each other (inter-symbol interference) and capping the bit rate over distance.
- **Crosstalk** — coupling between adjacent conductors (the reason twisted pair is *twisted*).

## The three media

| Medium | Reach / typical | Strengths | Weaknesses |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Twisted pair** (Cat5e/6/6A/8) | ~100 m | Cheap, ubiquitous, PoE | Attenuation & crosstalk limit distance; EMI-prone |
| **Coaxial** | Longer than UTP | Good shielding, broadband (DOCSIS) | Bulky; largely legacy for LANs |
| **Optical fibre** | Up to ~80 km+ unamplified | Enormous bandwidth, no EMI, low loss (~0.2 dB/km) | Cost, fragility, fusion-splicing skill |
| **Wireless** | Varies hugely | No cabling, mobility | Shared/contended spectrum, fading, interference |

<Callout variant="insight" title="Single-mode vs. multi-mode fibre">
**Multi-mode fibre (MMF)** has a wide core, so light takes many paths ("modes") that arrive at slightly
different times — **modal dispersion** limits it to short reach (data-center rows, driven by cheap
VCSELs). **Single-mode fibre (SMF)** has a core so narrow that light takes essentially one path,
eliminating modal dispersion — so with a precise laser at 1310/1550 nm it carries enormous bandwidth for
tens of kilometers. Long-haul and metro are SMF; inside-the-rack is often MMF. The remaining limit on
SMF is **chromatic dispersion**, managed with dispersion-compensating fibre and coherent optics.
</Callout>

## Multiplexing: sharing one medium

A single fibre or cable can carry many independent conversations at once. Three ways to slice it:

- **TDM (Time-Division)** — give each stream a recurring **time slot**. The basis of the
  telephone/transport hierarchy (T1/E1, **SONET/SDH**). **Statistical TDM** hands idle slots to whoever
  has data, the packet-switching idea that underlies Ethernet and the internet.
- **FDM (Frequency-Division)** — give each stream its own **frequency band** on the same medium
  simultaneously. Analog radio and legacy cable TV; OFDM is FDM taken to many tiny orthogonal carriers.
- **WDM (Wavelength-Division)** — FDM for light: send many **wavelengths (colors)** down one fibre at
  once, each a fully independent channel.

<Callout variant="insight" title="DWDM: terabits down a single glass strand">
**Dense WDM** packs 80+ wavelengths onto one fibre on a precise ITU grid (e.g. 50 GHz spacing around
1550 nm), each carrying 100–800 Gb/s with coherent optics — **multiple terabits per second on a single
strand**, amplified all-optically by EDFAs every ~80 km without converting back to electronics. This is
the physical foundation of the internet backbone and submarine cables: when traffic grows, operators
"light up" more wavelengths on fibre that's already in the ground. **CWDM** is the cheaper, coarse-grid
cousin for shorter metro/access links.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms rooted in the medium">
- **Errors that worsen with cable length** — attenuation or dispersion exceeding the medium's budget;
  shorten the run, add a repeater, or move to fibre.
- **Intermittent copper errors near motors / fluorescent lights** — EMI coupling into unshielded pairs;
  use shielded cable or fibre, which is immune.
- **A fibre link that won't come up or has high loss** — a dirty or damaged connector end-face
  (the #1 fibre fault), a bad splice, or exceeding the optical power budget. Inspect and clean before
  anything else.
- **MMF link fails at a distance where SMF would work** — modal dispersion; the reach simply exceeds
  multi-mode's limit for that rate.
</Callout>
