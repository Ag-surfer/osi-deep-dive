## From a bit pipe to a usable link

The Physical layer hands up an unstructured stream of bits — no boundaries, no addressing, no error
checking. The Data Link layer makes that stream usable on a single link. It **frames** the bits (marks
where messages start and end), **addresses** them with MAC addresses so the right NIC picks them up,
**detects corruption** with a checksum, and on shared media **arbitrates** who may transmit. It is
split into two sublayers: **LLC** (multiplexing upper protocols) and **MAC** (addressing + media
access).

## MAC addresses

A MAC address is **48 bits**, written as six hex bytes (`a4:5e:60:1f:cd:02`). The top 24 bits are an
**OUI** assigned to the manufacturer; the bottom 24 identify the specific NIC. Two bits in the first
byte are special: the **I/G bit** (individual vs. group/multicast) and the **U/L bit** (universally vs.
locally administered). The all-ones address `ff:ff:ff:ff:ff:ff` is the **broadcast** — every NIC on the
segment accepts it.

<Callout variant="insight" title="MAC is flat; IP is hierarchical">
A MAC address says *who* a NIC is, not *where* it is — there's no structure to aggregate or route on.
That's why it only works within one link/broadcast domain, and why the Network layer's hierarchical IP
addressing is needed to cross between networks.
</Callout>

## Error detection: parity, checksums, and the power of CRC

The wire flips bits — rarely on fiber, routinely on radio. The Data Link layer's defense has a clear
hierarchy of strength:

- **Parity bit** — append one bit making the count of 1s even. Catches any single-bit error; *any
  two* flips cancel out and slip through.
- **Internet checksum** — sum the data in 16-bit words (used by IP/TCP/UDP for speed in software).
  Weak: reordered words and several classes of paired flips go undetected.
- **CRC** (cyclic redundancy check) — treat the whole frame as one giant binary polynomial, divide it
  by an agreed **generator polynomial**, and append the remainder. The receiver repeats the division:
  a non-zero remainder means corruption. Ethernet's 32-bit FCS is exactly this.

CRC's strength is mathematical, not heuristic: at Ethernet frame lengths, CRC-32 detects **all**
errors of up to three flipped bits, and **every burst error up to 32 bits long** —
precisely the error shape real channels produce (a noise spike corrupts a *run* of adjacent bits, not
random isolated ones). That fit between math and physics is why CRC owns Layer 2 while the simpler
checksum survives only where software speed once mattered.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Detection vs. correction — and who retransmits">
Detecting an error needs far less redundancy than fixing one (correction codes like **Hamming** or
modern **FEC** must locate the flipped bit, not just notice it). Wired links choose the cheap option:
detect, **silently drop** the frame, and let TCP's retransmission recover end-to-end. Noisy radio
links can't afford that — Wi-Fi adds **link-layer ACKs and retransmission**, and 5G/satellite add FEC
— because fixing errors locally beats paying a full end-to-end round trip per loss. Where recovery
lives is an engineering choice about *where errors are cheapest to fix*.
</Callout>

## Media access control: who transmits when?

On a shared medium, two stations transmitting at once **collide**. Two classic strategies:

- **CSMA/CD** (classic half-duplex Ethernet): *Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision
  Detection*. Listen first; if idle, transmit while still listening. If you detect a collision, send a
  **jam** signal and back off a random time (binary exponential backoff) before retrying.
- **CSMA/CA** (Wi-Fi): you **cannot** reliably detect collisions on radio, so 802.11 tries to *avoid*
  them — random backoff before sending, and optional RTS/CTS reservation.

### Why radio is harder: hidden terminals

Wi-Fi can't simply reuse Ethernet's trick, for a physical reason: a radio **cannot hear a collision
while transmitting** (its own signal drowns out everything else), and worse, carrier sensing itself
breaks down. Picture laptops A and C on opposite sides of access point B, each in range of B but not
of each other. A senses the air, hears silence, and transmits — while C, equally convinced the
channel is free, does the same. Their frames collide *at B*, and neither sender ever knows. A and C
are **hidden terminals** to each other.

<LayerDiagram id="datalink-hidden-terminal" />

802.11's answers stack up: **CSMA/CA** waits a random backoff *before* transmitting (avoidance, since
detection is impossible); every unicast frame gets a **link-layer ACK**, so a collision is at least
*discovered* and retransmitted locally; and the optional **RTS/CTS** handshake lets a sender reserve
the channel — the short CTS from the access point is heard by *everyone in the AP's range*, including
stations the sender can't hear, silencing them for exactly the reserved duration.

<Callout variant="note" title="The exposed-terminal twin">
The mirror problem: B transmitting to A makes C (in B's range) stay silent even though C's intended
receiver D would hear C perfectly. Hidden terminals cause collisions; **exposed terminals** waste
capacity. Together they're why "just sense the carrier" — sufficient for a wire — needed a decade of
MAC-protocol research to survive contact with radio.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="history" title="Why collisions are now history">
Modern switched, full-duplex Ethernet gives every device its own dedicated collision-free link.
CSMA/CD is effectively a museum piece on wired LANs — but it's the reason Ethernet scaled, and the
ideas live on in Wi-Fi's CSMA/CA.
</Callout>

<WorkedExample title="On-the-wire efficiency of a minimum-size frame">
Ethernet enforces a **64-byte minimum frame** (so collisions are detectable), and between frames the
medium needs an **8-byte preamble/SFD** plus a **12-byte inter-frame gap**.

1. A minimum frame carries only **46 bytes** of payload (18 bytes are header + FCS).
2. Total bytes occupied on the wire: `64 + 8 + 12 = 84` bytes.
3. Efficiency = `46 / 84 ≈ 55%`.

So tiny packets waste nearly half the wire. A **maximum** 1500-byte payload (1518-byte frame) gives
`1500 / (1518 + 20) ≈ 97.5%` — which is why bulk transfers love big frames (and why "jumbo frames"
exist).
</WorkedExample>

## Switching and ARP

A **switch** is the defining Layer-2 device. It **learns**: when a frame arrives, it records
`source MAC → port` in its **CAM/forwarding table**. To forward, it looks up the destination MAC and
sends the frame out only that one port (unknown destinations are **flooded** to all ports). This
confines traffic and gives each link its own collision domain. Loops between switches are broken by
the **Spanning Tree Protocol (STP)**.

When a host knows a destination's **IP** but needs its **MAC** to build a frame, it uses **ARP**

## Carving up and protecting the LAN: VLANs and Spanning Tree

Two Layer-2 mechanisms shape every real network you'll touch:

**VLANs** (IEEE 802.1Q) slice one physical switch into multiple **virtual
LANs** — separate broadcast domains that can't hear each other even on shared hardware. A 4-byte tag
in the Ethernet frame carries a 12-bit VLAN ID; **access ports** belong to one VLAN (the tag never
reaches the host), while **trunk ports** carry many VLANs between switches, tags intact. Finance and
guest Wi-Fi can share every cable and switch in the building yet be as isolated as if they were
separate networks — crossing between them requires a router, where policy can be enforced.

**Spanning Tree (STP)** solves a darker problem: Ethernet frames carry **no TTL**, so two switches
connected in a loop will circulate a single broadcast frame *forever*, multiplying it until the LAN
melts — a **broadcast storm**. STP lets switches elect a root, map the topology, and deliberately
**block** redundant links, turning any physical mesh into a loop-free logical tree. The blocked links
sit in reserve and activate when an active link fails — redundancy without loops.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why a TTL-less world needs a tree">
IP packets carry a TTL (typically starting at 64–128) and die when it runs out; Ethernet frames never expire. That single design difference
is why routing tolerates loops (they self-heal as packets die) while switching cannot afford even one.
When someone plugs both ends of a cable into the same switch and the floor's network dies, you're
watching that difference in action — and watching what STP exists to prevent.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms at Layer 2">
- **Broadcast storm** — a switching loop with no/failed STP; broadcasts multiply until the LAN melts.
- **MAC flapping** — the same MAC seen on two ports (a loop or a duplicated address); the table churns.
- **Duplex mismatch** — one side full-, one side half-duplex → late collisions and miserable
  throughput that *looks* like a Physical problem.
- **VLAN misconfig** — a host in the wrong VLAN can reach nothing beyond its (empty) broadcast domain.
</Callout>

## Security considerations

<Callout variant="security" title="Attack surface at Layer 2">
- **ARP spoofing/poisoning** — ARP has no authentication; an attacker answers ARP requests with its own
  MAC to become a man-in-the-middle. Defense: **Dynamic ARP Inspection**, static ARP for critical hosts.
- **MAC flooding (CAM overflow)** — flood bogus source MACs until the switch table overflows and the
  switch fails open, flooding all traffic (so the attacker can sniff). Defense: **port security**.
- **VLAN hopping** — abusing trunk negotiation or double-tagging to reach other VLANs. Defense: disable
  DTP, prune trunks, avoid the native VLAN.
- **Rogue DHCP / 802.1X** — port-based network access control (802.1X) authenticates devices before
  granting link access.
</Callout>
