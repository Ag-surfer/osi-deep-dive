## A database wearing a phone book costume

Calling DNS "the internet's phone book" undersells it: it's a **globally distributed, delegated,
eventually-consistent database** that answers ~quadrillions of queries a year with millisecond
latency, administered by millions of independent parties who never coordinate. Every connection
starts here — which is why its failures masquerade as total outages, and why "it's always DNS" is
both a joke and a statistics lesson.

## The architecture: delegation all the way down

The namespace is a tree (`www.example.com.` — note the implicit root dot), and **authority is
delegated along it**: the **root** (13 named servers, over a thousand anycast instances) delegates
`.com` to the TLD servers; `.com` delegates `example.com` to whoever the registrar recorded;
your **authoritative** servers answer for your zone. Nobody holds the whole database — each zone's
operator holds their slice, and **NS records** are the pointers between slices.

Resolution splits roles: your device's **stub resolver** asks a **recursive resolver** (your
ISP's, or 8.8.8.8/1.1.1.1), which walks the tree — root → TLD → authoritative — **iteratively**,
then caches everything it learned. The cache is the whole performance story: the root isn't
consulted per lookup; it's consulted when the resolver's `.com` NS entry (TTL: days) expires.

## The records that run the world

| Type | Maps name to… | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A / AAAA | IPv4 / IPv6 address | The workhorses |
| CNAME | Another name | Alias; cannot coexist with other data at the same name (the apex problem) |
| MX | Mail server + preference | Why email survives server moves |
| TXT | Arbitrary text | Where SPF/DKIM/DMARC and domain-ownership proofs live |
| NS | The zone's authoritative servers | The delegation mechanism itself |
| SOA | Zone metadata | Serial, refresh, and the **negative-caching TTL** |
| PTR | Address to name (reverse) | Mail-server reputation checks |
| SRV / HTTPS | Service location, ports, ALPN | HTTPS records are how clients discover HTTP/3 |

**TTLs are the operational control surface.** Long TTLs absorb load and survive outages; short
ones enable fast failover at the price of constant queries. The migration ritual — lower TTL to
60 s a day before, change the record, raise it after — exists because *caches obey the TTL that
was on the record when they fetched it*. And **negative caching** (SOA-governed) means NXDOMAIN
answers linger too: a typo'd record stays broken for that long after you fix it.

<WorkedExample title="Debugging with dig like you mean it">
`dig +trace www.example.com` reproduces the resolver's walk: root referral → `.com` referral →
authoritative answer. Three follow-ups localize any DNS incident:

1. `dig @1.1.1.1 www.example.com` vs `dig @<your-resolver>` — records vs resolver problem.
2. `dig @ns1.example.com www.example.com` — ask the authoritative server directly: is the zone
   itself right?
3. Check the `flags`: `aa` = authoritative answer; missing `ra` = that server won't recurse for
   you; `SERVFAIL` vs `NXDOMAIN` distinguish "resolution broke" from "name doesn't exist."
</WorkedExample>

## Transport and privacy: the modern shifts

Classic DNS is UDP/53 with TCP fallback (the **TC** truncation bit) — and TCP was always required
for zone transfers. Today three encrypted transports are mainstream: **DoT** (TLS on 853), **DoH**
(HTTPS — indistinguishable from web traffic, RFC 8484), and DNS over QUIC. They move trust rather
than remove it: your ISP can no longer watch queries, the DoH provider now can. Enterprise
security teams have the mirrored complaint — DoH bypasses their monitoring — hence "canary
domains" and managed-browser policies.

<Callout variant="security" title="Poisoning, DNSSEC, and the gap nobody closed">
**Cache poisoning** (Kaminsky, 2008): race the real answer with forged responses; if your guess of
the query ID lands first, the resolver caches your lie for the TTL. Mitigations raised the bar
(random IDs + random source ports); **DNSSEC** closes it cryptographically — zones sign records
(RRSIG), parents publish child key digests (DS), validators walk the chain from the root. The
catch: deployment is a minority of zones, validation failures look like outages, and DNSSEC signs
*authenticity, not privacy* — it proves the answer is genuine while doing nothing to hide the
question. Privacy is DoH/DoT's job; the two are complements, not rivals.
</Callout>
