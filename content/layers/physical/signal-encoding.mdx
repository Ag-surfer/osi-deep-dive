## From bits to signals

The [Physical layer](../../physical/) has one job: put a stream of bits onto a medium as a physical
signal, and recover them at the far end. The naive approach — "1 = high voltage, 0 = low" (**NRZ-L**) —
works until you ask the hard question: **how does the receiver know where one bit ends and the next
begins?** Its clock must stay aligned with the sender's, sampling once per bit. Send `00000000` as a
flat low voltage and the receiver, with a slightly fast or slow clock, **loses count** — was that 7
zeros or 8? Line coding exists to solve this clock-recovery problem (and a second one, DC balance).

<Callout variant="insight" title="The two properties a good line code provides">
- **Self-clocking** — frequent, guaranteed signal **transitions** let the receiver recover the clock
  from the data itself, so no long run of identical bits can drift it out of sync.
- **DC balance** — equal time high and low (no net bias), so the signal survives **AC-coupled** paths
  (transformers, capacitors) that block a DC offset, and the receiver's threshold doesn't wander.
NRZ-L has neither; everything below is a way to buy them, trading some bandwidth for reliable recovery.
</Callout>

## The classic schemes

Step through the same bit string under four encodings and watch where each places its transitions —
note how Manchester refuses to sit still even through a run of identical bits, while NRZ-L goes flat:

<LineCodingViz bits="0111110" />

- **NRZ-L** — level *is* the bit. Maximally efficient (signal rate = bit rate) but no clocking and no DC
  balance; unusable alone for long runs.
- **NRZI** — a **1 causes a transition**, a 0 holds. Transitions now depend on data, not absolute level
  (robust to polarity inversion). Used by **USB** (with bit stuffing — insert a forced transition after
  six 1s — to guarantee clocking).
- **Manchester** — a transition in the **middle of every bit** (used by 10 Mb Ethernet). Perfectly
  self-clocking and DC-balanced, but it doubles the signaling rate: a 10 Mb/s link signals at 20
  Mbaud. That bandwidth cost is why it didn't scale past 10 Mb.
- **Differential Manchester** — always a mid-bit transition for clocking; the **bit is encoded by the
  presence/absence of a transition at the bit's start** (used by Token Ring). Polarity-independent.

## Block codes: clocking without doubling the bandwidth

Manchester's 100% overhead is too expensive at speed. **Block codes** map small groups of data bits to
slightly larger symbols chosen to guarantee transitions and DC balance — far cheaper than doubling:

| Code | Maps | Overhead | Used by |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **4B/5B** | 4 data bits → 5-bit symbol | 25% | 100BASE-TX (Fast Ethernet), FDDI |
| **8B/10B** | 8 data bits → 10-bit symbol | 25% | 1G Ethernet, PCIe gen1–2, Fibre Channel, SATA |
| **64B/66B** | 64 data bits → 66-bit frame | ~3% | 10G Ethernet and up (with a scrambler) |
| **128B/130B** | 128 bits → 130 | ~1.5% | PCIe gen3+ |

<Callout variant="note" title="The shift from coding to scrambling">
4B/5B and 8B/10B use a **lookup table** of hand-picked symbols that always contain transitions and stay
DC-balanced — robust, but the 25% overhead hurts at 10 Gb/s. **64B/66B** instead prepends a 2-bit
preamble (guaranteeing one transition per 66 bits) and runs the payload through a **self-synchronizing
scrambler** that statistically breaks up long runs. You trade a guarantee for a near-certainty and
recover ~22% of the wire — which is why everything fast (10/40/100G) uses scrambled 64B/66B, not block
tables.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="history" title="And beyond two levels: PAM4">
At 100G-per-lane and above, even clever binary coding hits the wall of how fast you can toggle a wire.
Modern links move to **PAM4** — four voltage levels carrying **2 bits per symbol** — halving the symbol
rate for a given bit rate. That's the bridge from line coding into true
[modulation](../modulation-capacity/): once you encode multiple bits per symbol, you're trading signal
levels (and noise margin) for bandwidth, exactly as Shannon's theorem describes.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms rooted in encoding/signal integrity">
- **Loss of sync / bursts of errors on long data runs** — a clocking failure; an encoding without
  guaranteed transitions (or a broken scrambler) drifting out of bit-lock.
- **Baseline wander** — a DC-imbalanced signal through an AC-coupled path drifting the decision
  threshold, causing errors that worsen with certain data patterns.
- **Symbol/alignment errors after a clean physical link** — code-group or block-lock failure (e.g.
  8B/10B disparity errors), often a marginal transceiver or a speed/encoding mismatch.
</Callout>
