## Authentication vs. authorization

Two words often blurred, and the whole of access control depends on keeping them apart:

- **Authentication (authN)** — proving **who you are** (the right password, key, or ticket).
- **Authorization (authZ)** — deciding **what you may do** once your identity is established.

This page is about authN: the harder, more fundamental problem of proving identity **across an untrusted
network**, where an eavesdropper sees every byte and an impostor can replay what they capture.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why 'just send the password' fails">
Sending a password over the network exposes it to anyone on the path, and even encrypting it doesn't
stop a **replay** — capture the encrypted login and resend it later. And making every service store every
user's password multiplies the places a breach can leak them. A good scheme must prove identity **without
transmitting the secret**, **resist replay**, and **avoid spreading credentials** across services.
Kerberos solves all three with a trusted third party and time-limited tickets.
</Callout>

## Kerberos: tickets from a trusted third party

Kerberos (named for the three-headed dog guarding Hades — for its three parties) centers on a **Key
Distribution Center (KDC)** that every user and service already shares a secret key with. The KDC has two
faces: the **Authentication Server (AS)** that issues a **Ticket-Granting Ticket (TGT)**, and the
**Ticket-Granting Server (TGS)** that exchanges a TGT for per-service tickets. Step through the exchange:

<Callout variant="insight" title="The three things this buys you">
- **No password on the wire** — the AS encrypts its reply with a key derived from the user's password.
  Only the genuine user can decrypt it; that *successful decryption* is the proof, so the password itself
  never travels and no replay of a login works.
- **Single sign-on** — the TGT is obtained **once**. Every subsequent service is reached by exchanging
  the TGT for a service ticket at the TGS, with no further password prompts.
- **Services never see your password** — a service validates a ticket using its **own** key shared with
  the KDC. It trusts the KDC's vouching, so it needn't store or ever handle user credentials.
Kerberos is the engine behind **Active Directory** logon and most enterprise SSO.
</Callout>

## What Kerberos depends on

Its guarantees rest on two requirements worth knowing:

- **Synchronized clocks** — tickets and authenticators carry timestamps to defeat replay, so all hosts
  must agree on the time within a small window (default 5 minutes). Clock skew is the classic Kerberos
  failure.
- **A trusted, available KDC** — the KDC knows every secret, so it is both the **root of trust** and a
  **single point of failure**; it's replicated for availability and guarded intensely, because owning it
  owns the realm.

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms rooted in Kerberos">
- **'Clock skew too great' (KRB_AP_ERR_SKEW)** — host clocks differ beyond the allowed window; fix time
  sync (NTP/[PTP](../../physical/clocking-sync/)).
- **Authentication works then fails after hours** — ticket/TGT **lifetime** expired; the client must
  renew or re-authenticate.
- **All logons fail across the realm** — the **KDC is unreachable** (or DNS to it is broken); KDC
  availability is critical infrastructure.
- **Works for one service, not another** — a missing or mismatched **service principal name (SPN)** so
  the TGS can't issue (or the service can't validate) the ticket.
</Callout>

## Security considerations

<Callout variant="security" title="When the trust model is abused">
- **Golden / silver tickets** — an attacker who steals the KDC's master key (the `krbtgt` account) can
  forge TGTs (golden) or service tickets (silver) for anyone. Protect and rotate that key; KDC
  compromise is game-over for the realm.
- **Pass-the-ticket** — stealing a valid ticket from a compromised host and reusing it; limit ticket
  lifetimes and protect endpoint memory.
- **Weak password-derived keys** — offline cracking of captured AS material (AS-REP / Kerberoasting of
  service tickets); enforce strong keys/passwords and AES encryption types.
- **Single point of trust** — because the KDC holds every secret, its security *is* the realm's security.
</Callout>
