## The glue between two address worlds

You have a destination IP; to put a frame on the wire you need a destination **MAC**. ARP is the
translator — and it sits in exactly the seam the OSI model draws between Layer 3 (logical, global)
and Layer 2 (physical, local). It's tiny (RFC 826 is from 1982 and barely changed), it runs
constantly under every IPv4 conversation, and its complete absence of authentication makes it one
of the most reliable attack surfaces on any LAN.

## The mechanism

Two messages on the wire (a request and a reply), wrapped in cache operations:

1. Host A wants `192.0.2.5`'s MAC. It checks its **ARP cache** first; on a miss it **broadcasts**
   (to `ff:ff:ff:ff:ff:ff`): *"Who has 192.0.2.5? Tell 192.0.2.3."* Every host on the segment
   receives it.
2. Only the owner of `.5` replies — **unicast**: *"192.0.2.5 is at `a4:5e:60:…`."*
3. A caches the mapping (minutes-long timer) and sends its frame. Everyone else who saw the
   broadcast may opportunistically cache A's mapping too.

The scoping rule is the load-bearing insight: **ARP only works within a broadcast domain.** For a
remote destination, the host doesn't ARP for the server at all — it ARPs for its **default
gateway** and ships the frame there. This is why the [subnet mask](/layers/network/) matters so
much: it's what decides "local, so ARP for it" vs "remote, so ARP for the router."

## Gratuitous ARP: the same packet, three jobs

An unsolicited ARP — a host announcing its own mapping to nobody in particular — is mechanically
identical to an attack and operationally essential:

- **Announcement**: "I'm here, update your caches" after boot or an IP change.
- **Duplicate detection** (RFC 5227): an ARP *probe* for your own address with sender IP `0.0.0.0`
  (deliberately not gratuitous — the zero sender can't poison caches); any reply means a conflict.
- **Failover**: when a standby device assumes a virtual IP (VRRP, load-balancer HA, live VM
  migration), one gratuitous ARP instantly repoints every cache on the LAN to the new MAC — this
  is how sub-second failover *works*.

<WorkedExample title="Why your laptop reaches a server it never ARPed for">
Laptop `192.168.1.50/24`, gateway `.1`, fetching `93.184.216.34` (remote):

1. Mask says `93.184.216.34` is **not** on `192.168.1.0/24` → it's remote.
2. So the laptop doesn't ARP for the server (it couldn't — that broadcast never leaves the LAN).
   It ARPs for the **gateway** `192.168.1.1`.
3. Frame: destination **MAC = the router's**, destination **IP = the server's**. The router
   strips the frame, routes on the IP, and builds a fresh L2 frame for the next hop.
4. Every hop repeats this — new MACs each link, same IPs end to end. ARP is the per-hop ritual
   that makes global routing physically possible.
</WorkedExample>

## Security: the LAN's original sin

<Callout variant="security" title="ARP spoofing and its descendants">
ARP replies are believed unconditionally — there's no challenge, no signature, no state. Send
forged replies claiming the **gateway's IP** maps to **your MAC**, and every victim sends you their
internet traffic: a textbook on-path MITM (the engine inside tools like Ettercap and
`bettercap`), enabling sniffing, SSL-strip attempts, and selective drops. It also powers
**ARP-based DoS** (poison with a black-hole MAC). The defenses are switch-side because the
protocol can't defend itself: **Dynamic ARP Inspection** validates ARP against the DHCP-snooping
binding table and drops the liars; static ARP entries protect critical hosts; and the real
backstop is **encrypt everything above** (TLS), so a successful MITM yields ciphertext. IPv6
relives the whole drama with NDP — and the same medicine, RA Guard / SEND.
</Callout>
