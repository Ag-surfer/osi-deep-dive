## Where TLS sits

TLS provides a **secure channel** — confidentiality, integrity, and authentication — for application
data. In strict OSI terms its jobs straddle the **Session** and **Presentation** layers, but in the
TCP/IP world it rides directly on a Transport connection (TCP, or DTLS over UDP) and presents a
plain reliable stream upward, so we cover it here alongside its transport. HTTPS is simply HTTP over a
TLS channel on port 443.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Three guarantees, and what each defends">
- **Confidentiality** — eavesdroppers see only ciphertext (AEAD encryption).
- **Integrity** — any tampering is detected and the connection fails (the AEAD authentication tag).
- **Authentication** — you're talking to who you think (the server's **certificate**, validated against
  a trusted CA; optionally the client's too). Without authentication, encryption alone just gives you a
  private channel to an *attacker*.
</Callout>

## The record protocol

Once the handshake completes, everything is carried in **records**: application data is fragmented,
each fragment sealed with **AEAD** (Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data — e.g. AES-GCM or
ChaCha20-Poly1305) under keys derived in the handshake, and tagged so tampering is detected on receipt.
AEAD fuses encryption and integrity into one operation, which is why TLS 1.3 removed the old CBC-mode
**MAC-then-encrypt** cipher constructions that caused a decade of padding-oracle bugs (POODLE, Lucky13).
(Encrypt-then-MAC was a *mitigation* for those; AEAD makes the whole question moot.)

## The handshake

The handshake authenticates the peer and agrees on shared keys. **TLS 1.3 cut it to one round trip** and
made **forward secrecy mandatory** — a redesign worth understanding against 1.2.

<Callout variant="note" title="What 1.3 changed from 1.2">
- **1-RTT, not 2** — the client guesses the key-exchange group and sends its share immediately.
- **Forward secrecy is mandatory** — only **ephemeral** (EC)DHE key exchange survives. Static-RSA key
  transport (where compromising the server key later decrypts *recorded* past traffic) is gone.
- **The cipher menu was gutted** — no RC4, no CBC-mode MtE, no compression, no renegotiation. Only AEAD
  suites remain, eliminating whole classes of attack (BEAST, CRIME, Lucky13, POODLE).
- **More is encrypted** — the certificate and the rest of the handshake are encrypted, improving privacy.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="insight" title="Forward secrecy, concretely">
With **ephemeral** Diffie-Hellman, the session key is derived from one-time key pairs that are thrown
away after the handshake. An attacker who records the ciphertext today and **steals the server's private
key tomorrow** still cannot decrypt that recorded session — the ephemeral secrets are gone. This is the
single biggest reason 1.3 mandated (EC)DHE and removed RSA key transport.
</Callout>

## Certificates and the PKI trust chain

Authentication rests on a **chain of trust**: the server presents a **leaf** certificate (binding its
public key to its hostname), signed by one or more **intermediate** CAs, chaining up to a **root** CA
your OS/browser already trusts. The client validates the whole chain — signatures, **hostname match**
(<RFCRef n={6125} />), validity dates, and **revocation** (OCSP/CRL, ideally **OCSP stapling** so the
server proves freshness without a side connection). Two handshake extensions ride along:

- **SNI** (Server Name Indication) — the client names the host it wants in the ClientHello, so one IP
  can serve many TLS sites. (Encrypted SNI / ECH closes the privacy gap that plaintext SNI leaves.)
- **ALPN** — negotiates the application protocol (e.g. `h2`, `h3`) inside the handshake, saving a round
  trip and enabling HTTP/2 and HTTP/3.

**Certificate Transparency** (<RFCRef n={6962} />) requires CAs to log every certificate to public,
append-only logs, so a mis-issued certificate for your domain can be detected.

## Session resumption and 0-RTT

A full handshake is costly, so TLS lets a returning client **resume**. In 1.3, the server hands out a
**pre-shared key (PSK) ticket**; on return the client presents it and skips the certificate exchange.
Resumption can even carry **0-RTT "early data"** — application data sent in the *first* flight, before
the handshake finishes.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The 0-RTT replay trade-off">
0-RTT early data is **replayable**: an attacker can capture and resend that first flight, and the server
may process it twice. It's therefore safe only for **idempotent** requests (a GET), never for anything
with side effects (a payment). 0-RTT also weakens forward secrecy for that early data. Speed here is a
deliberate trade against security — enable it only where the semantics are safe.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms and the cause behind them">
- **NET::ERR_CERT_*  / "certificate not trusted"** — a missing intermediate in the chain, an untrusted
  root, or a self-signed cert. Servers must send the full chain (leaf + intermediates), not just the leaf.
- **Hostname mismatch** — the certificate's SAN doesn't cover the requested name (often a bare apex vs.
  `www`, or wrong SNI).
- **Expired certificate / clock skew** — validity is absolute time; a wrong client clock breaks
  otherwise-valid certs.
- **Handshake failure / no shared cipher** — a client/server with no overlapping protocol version or
  AEAD suite (e.g. an old client against a 1.3-only server).
- **Slow first byte** — full handshakes on every connection; enable resumption/tickets and OCSP stapling.
</Callout>

## Security considerations

<Callout variant="security" title="Defending the channel and the trust">
- **Downgrade attacks** — forcing a weaker version/suite; 1.3's handshake transcript hash and version
  signaling detect tampering, and **HSTS** forces HTTPS so a stripping attacker can't drop you to HTTP.
- **Certificate mis-issuance / rogue CA** — the PKI's weakest link; **Certificate Transparency** and
  CAA records constrain who may issue for a domain.
- **Private-key compromise** — bounded by **forward secrecy** (past sessions stay safe) and short-lived
  certificates plus prompt revocation.
- **Implementation bugs** — memory-safety flaws (Heartbleed) leaked keys despite correct protocol;
  defense is hardened, audited libraries, not protocol design alone.
</Callout>
