## What TCP guarantees, and how

The [Transport overview](../../transport/) introduces TCP's contract: a **reliable, ordered byte
stream** over IP's unreliable packets. That guarantee is assembled from four mechanisms working
together — **sequence numbers** (order + dedup), **acknowledgments + retransmission** (reliability),
the **sliding window** (flow control), and **congestion control** (network protection, covered on its
[own page](../congestion-control/)). This page is the machinery behind the contract.

## The connection lifecycle

TCP is connection-oriented: a three-way handshake synchronizes initial sequence numbers before any
data, and a four-way exchange tears each half of the stream down independently.

<TcpStateMachine />

<Callout variant="insight" title="TIME_WAIT: the most misunderstood state">
The side that sends the final ACK enters **TIME_WAIT for 2×MSL** (Maximum Segment Lifetime, ~1–4 min)
before fully closing. It exists for two reasons: (1) if that final ACK is lost, the peer will resend its
FIN and TIME_WAIT can re-ACK it; (2) it lets any **stray, delayed segments** from this connection die
out before the same 4-tuple could be reused, preventing them from corrupting a new connection. A busy
client or proxy opening many short connections can accumulate thousands of TIME_WAIT sockets and exhaust
ephemeral ports — tune with timestamps + `tw_reuse`, **not** by blindly lowering MSL.
</Callout>

## Reliability: sequence numbers, ACKs, and the retransmission timer

Every byte has a **sequence number**; the receiver returns a **cumulative ACK** naming the next byte it
expects. A sender retransmits when either signal fires:

- **Triple duplicate ACK** → **fast retransmit** (immediate; see [congestion control](../congestion-control/)).
- **Retransmission timeout (RTO)** expires with no ACK → resend and back off.

Setting the RTO well is subtle — too short and you retransmit needlessly; too long and recovery stalls.
TCP estimates it adaptively from measured round-trip times (<RFCRef n={6298} />):

<Callout variant="note" title="Jacobson/Karels RTO, and Karn's correction">
TCP tracks a smoothed RTT (**SRTT**) and its variation (**RTTVAR**), then sets
`RTO = SRTT + 4 × RTTVAR` — widening the timer when latency is jittery. **Karn's algorithm** adds a
crucial rule: **never sample RTT from a retransmitted segment**, because you can't tell whether the ACK
matched the original or the retransmission (an ambiguity that would corrupt the estimate). On each
timeout the RTO also **doubles** (exponential backoff) until an unambiguous sample resets it.
</Callout>

### SACK: fixing cumulative ACK's blind spot

A pure cumulative ACK can only say "I have everything up to byte N." If segments 2 **and** 5 are lost
out of 1–6, the sender learns only that 2 is missing and may needlessly resend 3–6. **Selective
Acknowledgment** (<RFCRef n={2018} />) lets the receiver report the *non-contiguous blocks* it actually
holds, so the sender retransmits **only** the true gaps.

## Flow control deep dive

The receiver advertises a **window** — how many more bytes it can buffer — and the sender keeps at most
that much unacknowledged data in flight (a **sliding window**). Two refinements matter at scale:

<SlidingWindow />

- **Window scaling** (<RFCRef n={7323} />) — the header's window field is only 16 bits (max 64 KB), far
  too small for a high-bandwidth, long-RTT path. A handshake-time **scale option** left-shifts the
  window up to 1 GB, so the pipe can stay full. Negotiated only in the SYNs — it can't be added later.
- **Zero-window + persist timer** — when the receiver's buffer fills it advertises a window of 0,
  pausing the sender. To avoid deadlock if the re-opening "window update" is lost, the sender sends
  periodic **zero-window probes** until the window reopens.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Nagle vs. delayed-ACK: the 40 ms stall">
**Nagle's algorithm** (<RFCRef n={896} />) withholds a small segment until prior data is ACKed, coalescing
tiny writes to fight the **small-packet (tinygram) problem**. **Delayed ACK** withholds an ACK briefly (~40–200 ms),
hoping to piggyback it on a reply. Together they can **deadlock**: the sender waits for an ACK before
sending its small segment, while the receiver delays that ACK waiting for data — a classic latency
spike in request/response protocols. Interactive apps set **`TCP_NODELAY`** to disable Nagle.
</Callout>

## MSS and path MTU

The **Maximum Segment Size** is the largest payload a segment carries; each side advertises its MSS in
the SYN (typically link MTU − 40 for IPv4/TCP headers). If a smaller MTU lurks mid-path, **Path MTU
Discovery** finds it — but if a firewall blocks the ICMP "fragmentation needed" messages PMTUD relies
on, you get a **PMTU black hole**: the handshake completes, small requests work, but large transfers
hang. **MSS clamping** at a tunnel boundary is the common fix.

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms and the field behind them">
- **Connection hangs on large transfers, small ones fine** — a PMTU black hole (ICMP filtered); clamp
  MSS or fix PMTUD.
- **Throughput capped far below link rate** — window scaling disabled or a small receive buffer on a
  high-BDP path; or persistent loss (see [congestion control](../congestion-control/)).
- **Periodic ~40 ms latency in a request/response app** — the Nagle/delayed-ACK interaction; set
  `TCP_NODELAY`.
- **Ephemeral port exhaustion on a proxy** — TIME_WAIT pileup from many short connections; enable
  timestamps + reuse rather than slashing MSL.
- **Connection resets (RST)** — peer refused/aborted, or a middlebox injected a RST; distinguish from
  silent firewall drops.
</Callout>

## Security considerations

<Callout variant="security" title="Attack surface of the TCP state machine">
- **SYN flood** — half-open connections exhaust the backlog; **SYN cookies** encode the connection
  state in the sequence number so no server state is held until the handshake completes.
- **RST / sequence-number injection** — forging a RST or data with a guessed sequence number to abort
  or hijack a session; mitigated by randomized initial sequence numbers and, decisively, by TLS above.
- **TIME_WAIT assassination** — a crafted RST prematurely clears TIME_WAIT, risking stale-segment
  corruption; TCP timestamps (PAWS) defend against old segments.
- **Off-path blind attacks** — the 4-tuple plus random ports and ISNs raise the guessing bar; on-path
  attackers still need encryption above to be stopped.
</Callout>
