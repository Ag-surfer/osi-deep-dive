## One wire, many networks

A switch is, by default, **one broadcast domain** — every host hears every ARP, every broadcast,
and lives in one flat security zone. VLANs break that apart in software: a 4-byte tag on the
Ethernet frame partitions one physical switch (or a whole campus) into many isolated virtual LANs
that **cannot reach each other without a router**. Finance and guest Wi-Fi share every cable and
switch in the building while being as separated as if they were on different continents. It's the
single most-used Layer-2 feature in enterprise networking.

## The 802.1Q tag

Four bytes inserted right after the source MAC:

| Field | Bits | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- |
| TPID | 16 | `0x8100` — marks the frame as tagged |
| PCP | 3 | Priority (Class of Service) — 8 levels for QoS |
| DEI | 1 | Drop-eligible under congestion |
| **VLAN ID** | **12** | The VLAN — **4,094 usable** (0 and 4095 reserved) |

That 12-bit ceiling — 4,094 — is the number that drives a thousand architecture decisions: it's
why multi-tenant clouds outgrew VLANs and reached for [VXLAN's](#vxlan-when-4094-isnt-enough)
24-bit space.

## Access ports, trunks, and the native-VLAN trap

- **Access ports** belong to exactly one VLAN. The host plugged in is blissfully unaware — frames
  arrive untagged; the switch adds the tag on ingress and strips it on egress. Your laptop has no
  idea it's in VLAN 30.
- **Trunk ports** carry many VLANs between switches (and to routers/hypervisors), tags intact, so
  one cable backhauls every VLAN. The link between two switches is almost always a trunk.
- **The native VLAN** is the one VLAN sent *untagged* on a trunk (legacy compatibility) — and it's
  a recurring security hole (see below). Best practice: set it to an unused, dead VLAN.

Crossing VLANs requires Layer 3. "**Router-on-a-stick**" (one trunk to a router with sub-
interfaces per VLAN) is the teaching example; in practice an **SVI** on a Layer-3 switch does
inter-VLAN routing at hardware speed.

<WorkedExample title="Why the moved desk lost the printer but kept the internet">
Desks relocated; the new switch ports were left in the default VLAN 1 instead of the office VLAN
20.

1. DHCP on VLAN 1 hands out a *different subnet* than VLAN 20 — first clue: wrong IP range.
2. The printer lives in VLAN 20's broadcast domain. ARP for it from VLAN 1 **never reaches it** —
   different broadcast domains, full stop. `ping` times out, ARP never resolves.
3. Internet still works because the router serves *both* VLANs upstream.

The fix is one line — set the access ports to VLAN 20 — but the symptom ("internet yes, local
resources no") is the unmistakable fingerprint of a VLAN-assignment mistake. Pure Layer-2
misconfiguration wearing a Layer-3 disguise.
</WorkedExample>

## VXLAN: when 4,094 isn't enough

<Callout variant="insight" title="Layer 2 stretched over Layer 3">
Clouds need millions of isolated tenant networks, often spanning data centers an IP network apart
— both impossible for 802.1Q. **VXLAN** (RFC 7348) wraps the entire Ethernet frame in **UDP** and
tunnels it over any routed IP underlay, with a **24-bit** network identifier (~16.7 million
segments). The mental model: an **underlay** (a boring, all-links-forwarding L3 leaf-spine fabric)
carrying an **overlay** (virtual L2 segments that think they're on one cable). It's the same
encapsulation trick as a VPN, and it's why your Kubernetes pods on different physical nodes believe
they share a subnet. VLANs for the campus you wire; VXLAN for the fabric you scale.
</Callout>

## Security

<Callout variant="security" title="VLAN hopping">
Two classic escapes. **Switch spoofing**: a port left in dynamic-trunk auto-negotiation can be
coaxed into becoming a trunk, handing the attacker every VLAN — disable DTP, set access ports
hard. **Double tagging**: a frame with two 802.1Q tags whose outer tag matches the trunk's native
VLAN; the first switch strips the outer (native = untagged) and forwards the inner tag onward,
landing the frame in a VLAN the attacker can't normally reach. The defense is the native-VLAN
hygiene above — never use VLAN 1 as native, never carry the native VLAN where it isn't needed.
VLANs isolate, but they were never a strong security boundary on their own.
</Callout>
