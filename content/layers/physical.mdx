## Where data becomes physics

Every byte you send ends its journey down the stack here, at the boundary between information and the
physical world. The Physical layer's job is brutally concrete: **put a stream of bits onto a medium so
that the other end recovers exactly the same bits.** It standardizes the mechanical (connectors,
pinouts), electrical/optical (voltage levels, light wavelengths, power), and timing characteristics —
plus the two ideas that make digital transmission possible: **line coding** and **modulation**.

<Callout variant="insight" title="The Physical layer has no addresses and no header">
Unlike every layer above it, Layer 1 adds no header of its own — there is nothing to address, because
it only moves a raw symbol stream between two endpoints of a single link. Its "vocabulary" is signals,
not packets.
</Callout>

## Line coding: turning bits into signals

A naive scheme — "high voltage = 1, low = 0" (**NRZ**, non-return-to-zero) — fails in practice: a long
run of identical bits gives a constant signal, and without transitions the receiver's clock **drifts**
and loses count. Line codes solve clock recovery and DC balance:

| Code | Idea | Trade-off |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **NRZ** | Level = bit value | Simple, but no self-clocking on long runs |
| **NRZI** | *Transition* = 1, no transition = 0 | Robust to polarity inversion |
| **Manchester** | Mid-bit transition encodes value (used in 10BASE-T) | Self-clocking, but needs 2× bandwidth |
| **4B/5B + MLT-3** | Map 4 data bits → 5 code bits to guarantee transitions | Used in 100BASE-TX; ~80% efficiency |
| **8b/10b** | 8 bits → 10 symbols, DC-balanced | Used in gigabit fiber (1000BASE-X)/PCIe/SATA |
| **PAM-4** | 4 voltage levels carry 2 bits/symbol | Doubles rate (used in 400G+ Ethernet) |

<SignalWire />

<Callout variant="note" title="Self-clocking is the whole point">
There is no separate clock wire on Ethernet. The receiver extracts timing **from the data signal
itself** — which is why guaranteeing frequent transitions (Manchester, 4B/5B, 8b/10b) matters so much.
</Callout>

## The two fundamental limits

How fast can a channel go? Two theorems bound it.

**Nyquist** (noiseless channel): the maximum symbol rate is `2B` symbols/s for bandwidth `B`. With `M`
distinguishable signal levels, the data rate is `2B · log₂(M)` bits/s. More levels = more bits/symbol —
until noise makes them indistinguishable.

**Shannon** (noisy channel): the absolute capacity ceiling is `C = B · log₂(1 + S/N)`, where `S/N` is
the signal-to-noise ratio. No coding scheme can beat it.

<WorkedExample title="Max data rate of a voice-grade line">
A telephone channel has bandwidth **B = 3,100 Hz** and SNR of **30 dB**.

1. Convert SNR from dB: `S/N = 10^(30/10) = 1000`.
2. Shannon capacity: `C = 3100 · log₂(1 + 1000) = 3100 · 9.97 ≈ 30,900 bits/s`.

So ~30 kbps is the theoretical ceiling on a 3.1 kHz, 30 dB line — which is exactly why classic dial-up
modems plateaued near 33.6 kbps. (56k modems cheated by making the downstream path partly digital,
sidestepping the analog quantization noise.)
</WorkedExample>

## What's actually on the wire: preamble and SFD

Before an Ethernet frame's bits arrive, the Physical layer sends a fixed **preamble** so the receiver
can lock its clock, followed by a **Start Frame Delimiter** that says "the frame begins now."

## Media and devices

- **Twisted pair** (Cat 5e/6/6a) — cheap, ubiquitous, up to ~100 m; twisting cancels interference.
- **Coaxial** — legacy LANs and cable broadband.
- **Optical fiber** — glass carrying light; **single-mode** for long haul (km), **multi-mode** for
  short reach. Immune to EMI, enormous bandwidth.
- **Wireless** — modulated RF; the "medium" is shared space, which pushes complexity up to the MAC.

Pure Layer-1 devices are "dumb": **repeaters** regenerate a degraded signal, and **hubs** are
multi-port repeaters that blindly copy bits to every port (one collision domain — which is why they're
obsolete, replaced by Layer-2 switches).

| Medium | Typical reach | Strengths | Watch out for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Twisted pair (Cat 6a) | 100 m | Cheap, easy to terminate, PoE | Crosstalk, EMI near power runs |
| Multi-mode fiber | ~550 m | Cheap optics for short hops | Modal dispersion limits distance |
| Single-mode fiber | tens of km | Enormous bandwidth, EMI-immune | Costlier optics, fragile bend radius |
| RF (Wi-Fi, 5G) | room → km | No cables at all | Shared medium, interference, eavesdropping |

## Autonegotiation: how both ends agree

Plug in a cable and the two transceivers hold a sub-second conversation — exchanging **link pulses**
that advertise the speeds and duplex modes each supports — then commit to the best common option.
That's why a gigabit laptop on an old switch silently runs at 100 Mb/s, and why a damaged pair makes
a "gigabit" run negotiate *down* rather than fail: 1000BASE-T needs all four twisted pairs, while
100BASE-TX needs only two.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The classic autonegotiation trap">
Hard-coding speed/duplex on one end while the other autonegotiates is the canonical self-inflicted
wound: the autonegotiating side can't hear preferences from a silent partner, falls back to
**half-duplex**, and the result is a working-but-miserable link riddled with late collisions. If a
link is mysteriously slow but *up*, check that both ends agree on what they negotiated.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="When the problem is physical">
- **No link light** — broken cable, wrong pinout, or unpowered transceiver. Start here before
  debugging anything higher.
- **High CRC/frame errors** — marginal cabling, EMI, exceeding the distance limit, or a duplex
  mismatch. Bits are flipping on the wire.
- **Attenuation & noise** — signal weakens over distance; beyond spec the SNR drops below what the
  line code can recover.
</Callout>

## Security considerations

<Callout variant="security" title="Attack surface at Layer 1">
- **Wiretapping / fiber tapping** — copper radiates and can be inductively tapped; even fiber can be
  bent to leak light. Defense is encryption at higher layers (you must assume the medium is hostile).
- **Jamming** — flooding a wireless channel with RF energy denies service; hard to stop, possible to
  locate.
- **Physical access** — an unsecured port or patch panel is a foothold. Port security and locked
  facilities are Layer-1 controls.
</Callout>
