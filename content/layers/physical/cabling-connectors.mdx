## The physical plant

Beneath every protocol is hardware you can hold: the cable, the connector, the pluggable optic, and —
increasingly — the power riding alongside the data. This is where the Physical layer is most concrete,
and where a surprising share of "network" faults actually live.

## Twisted-pair copper

Ethernet over copper uses four **twisted pairs**. The twisting is not cosmetic: it makes each pair a
**balanced** transmission line so that external interference hits both wires equally and cancels at the
receiver, and it limits **crosstalk** between pairs. Tighter twists and better shielding push the usable
frequency — and thus the data rate — higher.

| Category | Rated for | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Cat5e** | 1 GbE @ 100 m | The workhorse for a decade |
| **Cat6** | 1 GbE @ 100 m, 10 GbE @ ~55 m | Tighter twists, less crosstalk |
| **Cat6A** | 10 GbE @ 100 m | Shielding to tame alien crosstalk |
| **Cat8** | 25/40 GbE @ ~30 m | Data-center top-of-rack short runs |

<Callout variant="note" title="T568A vs. T568B, and why it rarely matters">
The two TIA pinouts just swap the green and orange pairs; a cable is **straight-through** if both ends
use the same standard. They interoperate fine as long as both ends match. (Modern gear with **Auto-MDIX**
even fixes a straight/crossover mismatch automatically, retiring the old crossover-cable ritual.) The
real wiring faults are **split pairs** — wires landed on the right pins but not kept as a twisted pair —
which pass a basic continuity test yet wreck high-speed links with crosstalk.
</Callout>

## Optical fiber

Where copper's distance and bandwidth run out, [fiber](../media-multiplexing/) takes over. The
operational details are the connectors and end-faces:

- **Connectors** — **LC** (small, the data-center default), **SC** (push-pull), **ST** (legacy bayonet),
  and **MPO/MTP** (a multi-fiber ribbon connector for parallel 40/100/400G optics).
- **Polish** — **UPC** (blue) for most data; **APC** (green, angled end-face) where back-reflection
  must be minimal (e.g. PON, RF-over-glass). Never mate UPC to APC.
- **Grades** — **OS2** single-mode for distance; **OM3/OM4/OM5** multi-mode for short, cost-sensitive
  runs.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The #1 fiber fault is a dirty end-face">
A single speck of dust on a connector end-face scatters light and can drop a link or raise its error
rate. **Inspect and clean before mating, every time.** More fiber outages trace to contamination than to
broken glass. Respect the **minimum bend radius**, too — kinking a fiber bleeds light out of the core.
</Callout>

## Pluggable transceivers

Modern switches don't hard-wire their optics; they take **hot-swappable transceivers** so one port can
be copper, short fiber, or long-haul DWDM depending on the module you insert. The form factor encodes
the speed:

| Form factor | Typical speed | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **SFP / SFP+ / SFP28** | 1 / 10 / 25 G | Access & server links |
| **QSFP+ / QSFP28** | 40 / 100 G (4 lanes) | Switch uplinks, spine |
| **QSFP-DD / OSFP** | 200 / 400 / 800 G | Modern data-center fabric |

Within a rack you often skip optics entirely: a **DAC** (Direct Attach Copper) is a fixed twinax cable
with transceiver ends — cheap and low-power for a few meters — while an **AOC** (Active Optical Cable)
embeds the optics for longer reach without handling loose fiber. Each module still runs the link's
[line coding](../signal-encoding/) (8B/10B, 64B/66B, or PAM4) internally.

## Power over Ethernet

PoE delivers DC power **and** data over the same twisted pairs, so an access point, IP phone, or camera
needs only one cable. The standards climbed in lockstep with device appetite:

| Standard | Name | Power at the device | Powers |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **802.3af** | PoE | ~12.95 W | VoIP phones, basic APs |
| **802.3at** | PoE+ | ~25.5 W | PTZ cameras, larger APs |
| **802.3bt** | PoE++ (Type 3/4) | ~51 / 71 W | Wi-Fi 6/7 APs, displays, thin clients |

A **PSE** (switch/injector) negotiates with the **PD** (powered device) before energizing the line, and
the switch's total **power budget** is shared across ports — over-subscribe it and devices fail to power
up even though the data links are fine.

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms rooted in the physical plant">
- **Link negotiates slow, or flaps under load** — a run over the category/length limit, a split pair, or
  a marginal connector; certify the cabling.
- **Fiber link down or high errors** — a dirty/damaged end-face (clean first), a UPC↔APC mismatch, a bend
  past the radius, or a power budget exceeded by distance/splices.
- **Optic won't link** — a transceiver speed/type or wavelength mismatch between ends, or a vendor lock.
- **PoE device won't power on** — exceeded switch power budget, a cable that can't carry the wattage, or
  a PD/PSE class mismatch — while the data link itself works perfectly.
</Callout>
