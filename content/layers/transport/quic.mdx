## Why rebuild transport over UDP?

TCP works, but it is **hard to change**. Its logic lives in OS kernels and is second-guessed by every
middlebox on the path, so new features (faster handshakes, better loss recovery) take a decade to
deploy — TCP has **ossified**. And combining TCP with [TLS](../tls/) layers two separate handshakes and
leaves one stubborn flaw: **transport-level head-of-line blocking**. QUIC's answer is to rebuild a
modern transport — reliable, congestion-controlled, and *always encrypted* — **in user space on top of
UDP**, where it can be shipped in an app update and evolve freely. It is the basis of **HTTP/3**.

<Callout variant="insight" title="UDP is the substrate, not the point">
QUIC doesn't use UDP for UDP's semantics — it reimplements reliability, ordering, flow control, and
congestion control itself. UDP is just the **lowest-friction way through the existing internet**: it
passes most middleboxes, and a user-space stack over UDP can be updated without touching the kernel.
QUIC is to UDP what TCP is to IP — a reliable transport built on a datagram service.
</Callout>

## Streams: multiplexing without head-of-line blocking

The defining win. HTTP/2 multiplexes many requests over **one** TCP connection — but TCP is a single
ordered byte stream, so **one lost segment stalls every multiplexed request behind it** until that one
segment is retransmitted. QUIC carries many **independent streams** within a connection, each with its
own ordering. A loss on stream 3 holds up only stream 3; streams 1, 2, and 4 keep flowing.

## The merged handshake

TCP+TLS runs two handshakes in sequence (TCP's SYN exchange, then TLS's). QUIC **fuses the transport
and cryptographic handshakes** (TLS 1.3 is built directly into QUIC, <RFCRef n={9001} />), so a
connection — transport parameters *and* session keys — is established in **one round trip**, or **zero**
for a returning client using a PSK.

<Callout variant="note" title="Always encrypted — including the transport itself">
QUIC encrypts and authenticates almost everything, including most of its own headers. Packet numbers,
ACKs, and stream framing are not visible to middleboxes — which is what *keeps QUIC evolvable* (boxes
can't depend on, and thus ossify, fields they can't see). The cost is **observability**: traditional
passive TCP monitoring doesn't work, pushing diagnostics to the endpoints.
</Callout>

## Connection IDs and migration

A TCP connection **is** its 4-tuple (source IP/port, dest IP/port) — change your IP (Wi-Fi → cellular)
and the connection dies. QUIC identifies a connection by an opaque **Connection ID** carried in each
packet, *independent of the IP/port*. So when your phone switches networks, the QUIC connection
**migrates** and survives — the download doesn't restart. Connection IDs are also rotated to stop
on-path observers from linking your traffic across paths.

## Loss recovery and congestion control

QUIC keeps TCP's congestion-control ideas — <RFCRef n={9002} /> specifies a NewReno-baseline controller
as its example but lets implementations plug in any controller, and real stacks commonly ship CUBIC or
BBR — but fixes a long-standing TCP measurement flaw:

<Callout variant="insight" title="Monotonic packet numbers kill retransmission ambiguity">
TCP reuses a segment's sequence number when it retransmits, so an ACK can't reveal whether it matched
the original or the retransmission — the ambiguity that forces [Karn's algorithm](../tcp/). QUIC gives
**every packet a new, strictly increasing packet number**, even retransmissions. An ACK therefore always
identifies exactly which transmission arrived, yielding **unambiguous, accurate RTT samples** and cleaner
loss detection. Stream data is re-sent in *new* packets, decoupling "what to deliver" from "which packet
carried it."
</Callout>

## HTTP/3

HTTP/3 (<RFCRef n={9114} />) is HTTP semantics mapped onto QUIC: each request/response pair gets its own
QUIC stream (so requests never head-of-line-block each other), and **QPACK** replaces HTTP/2's HPACK
header compression with a design that tolerates QUIC's out-of-order stream delivery. ALPN negotiates
`h3` during the handshake.

## Failure modes & trade-offs

<Callout variant="warning" title="Where QUIC bites">
- **UDP blocked or throttled** — some enterprise/captive networks drop UDP or rate-limit it; clients
  **fall back to TCP+TLS (HTTP/2)**, losing QUIC's benefits. Happy-eyeballs-style racing hides the delay.
- **Higher CPU cost** — user-space processing plus per-packet encryption is heavier than kernel TCP with
  hardware offload (though offload is catching up).
- **Amplification abuse** — an unvalidated handshake could let a spoofed client turn the server into a
  reflector; QUIC limits the server to ~3× the client's bytes until the address is validated.
- **Operational blind spots** — encrypted transport headers defeat legacy passive monitoring; you
  instrument at the endpoints (e.g. qlog) instead.
</Callout>
