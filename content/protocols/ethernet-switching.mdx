## The protocol that refused to die

Ethernet has buried every rival — Token Ring, FDDI, ATM — by being cheap, simple, and relentlessly
re-invented: same frame format from 10 Mb/s coax in 1980 to 800 Gb/s fiber today. The deeper
story is that "Ethernet" quietly changed species: the shared-medium, collision-managed protocol
became a **switched, full-duplex, point-to-point** system that kept the old frame as its
interface. Modern Ethernet *is* switching.

Details with operational teeth: the **64-byte minimum** (collision-detection legacy, padded
transparently), the **1500-byte MTU** (and jumbo frames at ~9000 inside data centers — must match
end-to-end or you get silent black holes), **EtherType** as the L2→L3 demux key, and the **FCS**
whose per-port error counters are your first physical-layer diagnostic.

## The learning switch: three rules, no configuration

A switch builds its forwarding table by eavesdropping on source addresses — the algorithm fits on
an index card:

1. **Learn**: on every arriving frame, record *source MAC → ingress port* (with an aging timer,
   ~5 min).
2. **Forward**: destination known → send out exactly that port (unless it's the ingress port —
   then discard: the destination is already on that segment).
3. **Flood**: destination unknown, broadcast, or (unregistered) multicast → copy to every port
   except the ingress.

The elegance: it's self-configuring, self-healing (entries age out), correct without any topology
knowledge — and the reply traffic teaches the switch what flooding didn't know.

<WorkedExample title="Trace four frames through a fresh switch">
Hosts A and B on ports 1 and 2; the table starts empty.

1. **A → B**: source A learned on port 1; destination B unknown → flooded to all other ports.
2. **B → A**: source B learned on port 2; destination A is in the table → unicast out port 1
   only.
3. **A → B** again: both known → unicast out port 2. Flooding has converged away.
4. B's NIC moves to port 5 and transmits: the table simply *re-learns* B at port 5 — and this
   same mechanism is why a **loop** is catastrophic: one broadcast circulating two paths makes B
   appear to "move" thousands of times a second (**MAC flapping**) while multiplying — see
   [Spanning Tree](/protocols/stp/).
</WorkedExample>

## Under the hood of a real switch

- **CAM/TCAM hardware** does the lookups at line rate — forwarding is ASIC work, not CPU work,
  which is why switches handle billions of packets per second.
- **Cut-through vs store-and-forward**: start transmitting after reading just the destination MAC
  (microseconds, but forwards corrupt frames) vs buffer-verify-forward (full FCS check). Trading
  floors buy the former; most networks run the latter.
- **Collision domains died, broadcast domains didn't**: every switched port is its own collision
  domain (full duplex — CSMA/CD is retired), but one switch is still **one broadcast domain** —
  every host hears every ARP request. Segmenting *that* is [VLANs'](/protocols/vlan/) job.
- **Link aggregation (LACP, 802.1AX)** bonds parallel links into one logical pipe — with the
  subtlety that a single flow hashes onto a single member link, so one elephant flow can't exceed
  one link's speed.

<Callout variant="security" title="Attacking the learning algorithm">
**MAC flooding**: blast frames from random source addresses until the CAM table fills; many
switches then flood *everything* (fail-open into hub mode) — the attacker now sees all traffic.
Defense: port security (limit MACs per port). **MAC spoofing**: impersonate a host's address to
hijack its frames — the reason 802.1X port authentication and DHCP-snooping-style controls exist.
The lesson generalizes: data-plane learning with no authentication is convenient *and* gullible.
</Callout>
