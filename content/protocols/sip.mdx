## The protocol that sets up the call

When you make a VoIP call, join a video conference, or your phone rings over a modern carrier
(VoLTE is SIP underneath), something has to find the other party, negotiate what media you'll
exchange, ring them, and tear it all down afterward. That's **SIP** — the **signaling** protocol
that *establishes, modifies, and ends sessions*. It is the Session layer made literal: SIP never
carries your voice; it carries the conversation *about* the conversation.

## Signaling vs media: the split that defines it

<Callout variant="insight" title="SIP sets up the call; RTP carries it">
This separation is the whole architecture. **SIP** (the signaling) negotiates and controls; the
actual audio/video flows over a *different* protocol, **RTP**, usually **directly between the two
endpoints** while SIP messages may have travelled through a chain of servers. So the control path
and the media path differ — which is why "the call connects but there's no audio" is the
signature SIP/NAT failure (signaling succeeded; media couldn't find a path), and why
troubleshooting VoIP means always asking *which* path broke.
</Callout>

SIP deliberately mimics HTTP — text-based, request/response, header-rich — so it reads familiarly:

```
INVITE sip:bob@example.com SIP/2.0
Via: SIP/2.0/UDP pc.example.org;branch=z9hG4bK776
From: Alice <sip:alice@example.org>;tag=1928
To: Bob <sip:bob@example.com>
Call-ID: a84b4c76e66710
CSeq: 314159 INVITE
Content-Type: application/sdp

[SDP body: "I offer Opus audio on port 49170, here's my IP"]
```

## The INVITE dialogue

A call setup is a recognizable three-step, with provisional responses keeping everyone informed:

The **SDP** body inside the INVITE/200 is the negotiation: an **offer/answer** exchange where each
side lists codecs, media types, and the IP/port to send RTP to, converging on a shared set.
Methods beyond INVITE round it out: **REGISTER** (tell your provider where to reach you — how your
phone "logs in"), **BYE**, **CANCEL**, **REFER** (transfer), **OPTIONS** (capability probe).

## Why NAT is SIP's nemesis

<Callout variant="warning" title="The IP-address-in-the-body problem">
SIP/SDP put **IP addresses inside the message body** ("send your audio to 192.168.1.50:49170") —
a private address that's meaningless once the packet crosses NAT. Signaling reaches the server,
but the media address it advertised is unroutable, so RTP has nowhere to go: **one-way or no
audio**. The fixes are a tour of NAT traversal: **STUN** (ask a public server "what's my external
address?" and advertise *that*), **TURN** (when STUN fails behind symmetric NAT, relay media
through a public server — reliable but costs bandwidth), and **ICE** (try every candidate
address — host, STUN-discovered, TURN-relayed — and pick whatever works). WebRTC inherited this
entire stack wholesale; it's the same machinery in the browser.
</Callout>

## Where it lives today

SIP quietly runs an enormous amount: enterprise PBX systems and **SIP trunking** (replacing
physical phone lines with IP), carrier **VoLTE/VoWiFi** (your "regular" mobile calls), and as the
conceptual ancestor of WebRTC's signaling. Security mirrors the rest of the stack: **SIPS** (SIP
over TLS) protects signaling, **SRTP** encrypts the media, and unsecured SIP endpoints are a
favorite target for toll fraud — attackers placing premium-rate calls on your dime.
