## Where OSPF sits

OSPF is a **link-state interior gateway protocol (IGP)** — it routes *within* a single autonomous
system, where BGP routes *between* them. If the [routing concepts](../routing-concepts/) page is the
theory, OSPF is the canonical link-state implementation: every router floods a description of its own
links, every router assembles the identical map, and every router independently runs **Dijkstra's SPF**
to compute its own shortest-path tree. No rumor, fast convergence, and — crucially — a design that
*scales* through areas.

<Callout variant="insight" title="The five things OSPF does, in order">
1. **Discover neighbors** with Hello packets on each interface.
2. **Form adjacencies** and synchronize the **Link-State Database (LSDB)** with them.
3. **Flood LSAs** so every router in the area holds an identical LSDB.
4. **Run SPF (Dijkstra)** over that LSDB to build a shortest-path tree rooted at itself.
5. **Install** the resulting best routes into the RIB/FIB, and reflood/recompute on any change.
</Callout>

## Neighbors and adjacencies

OSPF speaks directly to IP, **protocol 89**, using multicast `224.0.0.5` (AllSPFRouters) and
`224.0.0.6` (AllDRouters). **Hello** packets (every 10 s on broadcast links, 30 s on NBMA by default)
discover neighbors and act as keepalives — miss the **dead interval** (4× hello) and the neighbor is
torn down.

Two routers only become **neighbors** if a set of Hello parameters match exactly. A mismatch here is
the single most common "why won't OSPF come up?" cause:

<Callout variant="warning" title="Must match to form an adjacency">
- **Area ID** — interfaces must be in the same area.
- **Hello and Dead intervals** — must be identical.
- **Subnet / mask** — neighbors must share the IP subnet (OSPFv2).
- **Authentication** — type and key must match.
- **Stub/area flags** (E-bit, N-bit) — a stub router won't peer with a non-stub.
- **MTU** — must match, or the adjacency hangs in **EXSTART** (see troubleshooting).
</Callout>

Becoming neighbors (2-Way) is not the same as becoming **fully adjacent** (Full) — only fully adjacent
routers exchange the complete LSDB. The bring-up walks a state machine:

## DR/BDR election: taming the n² problem

On a multi-access segment (Ethernet) with *n* routers, forming a full mesh of adjacencies would need
`n(n−1)/2` of them, and every router would flood to every other — an LSA storm that scales quadratically.
OSPF elects a **Designated Router (DR)** and **Backup DR (BDR)** so the segment behaves like a hub: all
routers form Full adjacencies **only with the DR and BDR**, and the DR originates a single **Type-2
Network LSA** representing the segment.

- Election is by highest **interface priority** (default 1; priority 0 = never DR), tie-broken by
  highest **Router ID**.
- The election is **non-preemptive** — a higher-priority router added later does *not* seize the role.
- DROther↔DROther pairs deliberately rest in **2-Way**; that is healthy, not a fault.
- DR/BDR live on `224.0.0.6`; others send updates there and the DR refloods to `224.0.0.5`.

<Callout variant="note" title="Network types change the rules">
DR/BDR election only happens on **broadcast** and **non-broadcast (NBMA)** networks.
**Point-to-point** and **point-to-multipoint** links elect no DR (there's no n² problem on a P2P link).
Mismatched network types across a link (e.g. one side broadcast, one side P2P) is a classic adjacency
failure — Hellos are sent to the wrong place.
</Callout>

## The link-state database: LSA types

The LSDB is built from **Link-State Advertisements**. Knowing the LSA types *is* knowing how OSPF
scales and where information stops at area boundaries — each type has a defined originator and flooding
scope.

| Type | Name | Originated by | Flooding scope | Describes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1** | Router LSA | Every router | Within its area | The router's own interfaces, their costs and states |
| **2** | Network LSA | The DR | Within the area | Which routers are attached to a multi-access segment |
| **3** | Summary LSA | ABR | Into adjacent areas | An inter-area prefix (network reachability across areas) |
| **4** | ASBR Summary | ABR | Into adjacent areas | How to reach the ASBR that owns Type-5s |
| **5** | AS-External | ASBR | Whole AS (except stub/NSSA) | A route redistributed into OSPF from outside |
| **7** | NSSA External | ASBR in an NSSA | Within the NSSA | Externals in a not-so-stubby area (ABR translates to Type-5) |

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why Type 1 and 2 never leave the area">
Router (1) and Network (2) LSAs describe the *detailed topology* — and SPF runs on exactly those.
Keeping them inside the area is what bounds the LSDB and the SPF run. The ABR translates intra-area
topology into **distance-only Type-3 summaries** for other areas: neighbors learn *that* a prefix is
reachable and *at what cost*, but not the internal graph. That information hiding is the whole scaling
story.
</Callout>

## Areas and the two-level hierarchy

OSPF forces a strict two-level hierarchy: a **backbone area (Area 0)** that every other area must touch,
directly or through a **virtual link**. Routers are classified by where they sit:

- **Internal router** — all interfaces in one area.
- **ABR (Area Border Router)** — straddles Area 0 and one or more other areas; generates Type-3/4
  summaries and is the natural **summarization** point (`area range`).
- **ASBR (AS Boundary Router)** — injects external routes (redistribution) as Type-5 (or Type-7 in an
  NSSA).
- **Backbone router** — has at least one interface in Area 0.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The 'no transit through a non-backbone area' rule">
Inter-area traffic must pass through Area 0. Two non-backbone areas cannot exchange routes directly —
their ABRs only talk to each other across the backbone. If an area is physically detached from Area 0,
you bridge it with a **virtual link** through a transit area (a design smell, not a goal).
</Callout>

### Stub area types

Areas can suppress LSA types they don't need, trading granular external knowledge for a smaller LSDB
and a default route. This is core CCNP/expert material — what each type blocks:

| Area type | Blocks | Default route | Typical use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Standard** | nothing | none | Normal area with full visibility |
| **Stub** | Type 5 (externals) | ABR injects a default | Edge area that doesn't need external routes |
| **Totally stubby** | Types 3, 4, 5 | ABR injects a default | Cisco; minimal LSDB at the network edge |
| **NSSA** | Type 5 | optional | A stub area that still has its *own* ASBR (Type-7) |
| **Totally NSSA** | Types 3, 4, 5 | ABR injects a default | NSSA + inter-area summary suppression |

## SPF: Dijkstra over the LSDB

Once the LSDB is synchronized, each router runs **Dijkstra's shortest-path-first** with *itself* as the
root. It settles routers in increasing cost order, relaxing each newly-settled router's links, until
every reachable router is on the tree. The link metric is **cost = reference-bandwidth ÷ interface-
bandwidth** — beware the [reference-bandwidth trap](../routing-concepts/) (the 100 Mbps default ties all
fast links at cost 1).

When two paths tie on cost, OSPF installs **all** of them (**ECMP**) and load-shares per flow. SPF is
`O(E log V)`; areas keep `V` and `E` small enough that recomputation stays cheap even in large networks.

### Path preference and external metric types

A router may learn the same prefix several ways; OSPF prefers them in a fixed order regardless of cost:

**Intra-area (O)** → **Inter-area (O IA)** → **External Type 1 (E1)** → **External Type 2 (E2)**.

- **E2** (the default for redistributed routes) carries a **fixed** external metric — the internal cost
  to reach the ASBR is *ignored*. Two routers far apart see the same E2 cost.
- **E1** adds the internal cost-to-ASBR to the external metric, so it reflects true distance. Prefer E1
  when multiple ASBRs advertise the same external and you want the nearest one chosen.

## Convergence and scaling (expert tuning)

Link-state converges fast, but uncontrolled flooding/SPF can itself become the problem at scale:

- **SPF throttling** (`spf-timers`) — exponential backoff between SPF runs so a flapping link can't pin
  the CPU re-running Dijkstra.
- **LSA throttling / pacing** — rate-limit LSA generation and group refreshes; the periodic LSA refresh
  is every 1800 s, and an LSA ages out at **MaxAge 3600 s**.
- **Incremental SPF (iSPF)** — recompute only the affected subtree, not the whole tree.
- **BFD** — sub-second failure detection decoupled from the (coarser) Hello/Dead timers, so OSPF reacts
  in milliseconds without aggressive Hellos.
- **ABR summarization** (`area range`) — a single summary hides dozens of intra-area prefixes *and*
  stops their flaps from rippling into other areas. The most effective scaling lever OSPF has.

<Callout variant="history" title="OSPFv3: OSPF for IPv6">
**OSPFv3** (<RFCRef n={5340} />) is a near-rewrite for IPv6. It runs **per-link, not per-subnet**
(adjacencies form on link-local addresses, and an address mismatch no longer blocks them), moves
addressing *out* of the Router/Network LSAs into new **Type-8 Link** and **Type-9 Intra-Area-Prefix**
LSAs (so the topology and the addressing are decoupled), and originally delegated authentication to
**IPsec** rather than carrying its own. Same SPF, same area model — a cleaner separation of topology
from addressing.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms and the cause behind them">
- **Stuck in EXSTART/EXCHANGE** — almost always an **MTU mismatch**; the DBD with the larger MTU is
  dropped. Match MTUs or `ip ospf mtu-ignore`.
- **Neighbors never pass 2-Way (on Ethernet)** — usually *normal* DROther↔DROther behavior. Only the
  DR/BDR go Full. A genuine problem if it's a P2P link.
- **Adjacency never forms** — mismatched Hello/Dead timers, area IDs, subnet/mask, authentication, or
  stub flags. Walk the must-match list above.
- **Duplicate Router ID** — two routers with the same RID corrupt the LSDB; routes flap or vanish. RIDs
  must be unique AS-wide.
- **Suboptimal paths on fast links** — the reference-bandwidth trap; raise `auto-cost reference-
  bandwidth` consistently everywhere.
- **Constant SPF churn** — a flapping interface flooding LSAs; stabilize the link, enable SPF/LSA
  throttling, and summarize at the ABR to contain the blast radius.
</Callout>

## Security considerations

<Callout variant="security" title="Attack surface at the control plane">
- **Authenticate adjacencies** — OSPFv2 supports null, plaintext (useless), and **MD5/HMAC-SHA**
  cryptographic auth; OSPFv3 uses IPsec AH/ESP. Without it, anyone on the segment can inject LSAs and
  reshape routing.
- **`passive-interface`** on any interface with no legitimate OSPF neighbor (LANs facing hosts) — stops
  Hellos from leaking and blocks rogue-router peering.
- **TTL security / GTSM** — OSPF is link-local (TTL 1 for multicast), so off-link attackers can't reach
  it; enforce it.
- **Fabricated/maximum-age LSA injection** and **MaxAge flooding** are the classic link-state attacks —
  authentication plus filtering what you accept is the defense, exactly as on every other routing
  protocol.
</Callout>
