## The problem: congestion collapse

Flow control stops a fast sender from overrunning a slow *receiver*. **Congestion control** stops the
collective senders from overrunning the *network* — the routers and links between them. The distinction
is on the [Transport layer overview](../../transport/); here we go deep on the network half.

In **October 1986** the early internet suffered **congestion collapse**: a link between LBL and UC
Berkeley dropped from 32 kbit/s to **40 bit/s** — an ~800× collapse. Senders retransmitted lost packets,
which added more load, which caused more loss, in a ruinous feedback loop. Van Jacobson's response —
slow start and congestion avoidance — is the reason the internet still works at scale. TCP infers
congestion from the network's only honest signal (**loss**, and later **delay** and **ECN marks**) and
backs off *before* collapse.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Two windows; the sender obeys the smaller">
A TCP sender tracks **two** limits and may have at most `min(rwnd, cwnd)` bytes in flight:
- **rwnd** (receive window) — flow control; advertised by the receiver.
- **cwnd** (congestion window) — congestion control; the sender's *own* estimate of what the network
  can absorb. Never sent on the wire — it is inferred, not negotiated.
Throughput is `min(rwnd, cwnd) / RTT`, so on a healthy link cwnd is usually the binding constraint.
</Callout>

## Slow start and congestion avoidance

TCP grows cwnd in two regimes, split by the **slow-start threshold (ssthresh)**:

- **Slow start** — probe aggressively from cold. cwnd starts at the **initial window** (≈10 segments
  in modern stacks) and **doubles every RTT** (it rises by one MSS per ACK). "Slow" is historical
  irony — it's exponential. It runs until cwnd reaches ssthresh or a loss occurs.
- **Congestion avoidance** — once past ssthresh, grow *gently*: **+1 MSS per RTT** (additive increase),
  carefully probing for more bandwidth without provoking loss.

On loss, TCP reacts by **how bad** the signal is:

- **Triple duplicate ACK** (three dup-ACKs) — packets after the lost one still arrive, so the network
  is delivering: a *mild* signal. **Fast retransmit** resends immediately (no waiting for a timer), and
  **fast recovery** halves ssthresh and cwnd, then resumes congestion avoidance. This is the
  **multiplicative decrease**.
- **Retransmission timeout (RTO)** — no ACKs at all: a *severe* signal. ssthresh halves, but cwnd
  collapses all the way to **1** and slow start restarts from scratch.

<Callout variant="note" title="AIMD: why the sawtooth is the point, not a bug">
The repeating "additive increase, multiplicative decrease" shape **is** the algorithm. Additive
increase (+1) probes politely for spare capacity; multiplicative decrease (÷2) backs off hard the
instant congestion appears. AIMD is what makes competing flows **converge toward a fair share** of a
link — a gentle linear climb that everyone halves together settles into equilibrium, whereas additive-
decrease or multiplicative-increase schemes oscillate or starve. The sawtooth is the visible signature
of a network sharing itself fairly.
</Callout>

## The algorithm families

Loss-based Reno works, but it underperforms on modern high-bandwidth, long-RTT links (a single loss
halves a huge window) and it *fills buffers* by design. The major variants:

| Algorithm | Signal | Growth | Where it shines / hurts |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Reno / NewReno** | Loss (dup-ACK / timeout) | AIMD linear | The classic; NewReno fixes multi-loss recovery. Slow to fill large BDP pipes |
| **CUBIC** | Loss | **Cubic** function of time since last loss | Linux/Android default. Fills long-fat pipes fast; RTT-fair-ish but still buffer-filling |
| **BBR** | **Model**: measured bottleneck bandwidth × min-RTT | Paces to the estimated pipe | Ignores loss as a congestion signal; high throughput, low latency — but can be unfair to loss-based flows |
| **DCTCP** | **ECN** marks (fine-grained) | Proportional to mark fraction | Data-center fabrics with ECN-capable switches; very low queueing |

<Callout variant="insight" title="BBR changed the question">
Loss-based CC equates "a buffer somewhere is full" with "the network is congested" — so it deliberately
fills buffers until something drops, creating **bufferbloat** (huge queueing latency). **BBR** instead
models the path: it measures the **bottleneck bandwidth** and **minimum RTT** and paces just enough data
to keep the pipe full *without* filling the queue (operating near the "BDP" optimum). It treats loss as
possibly just noise, not proof of congestion — which is why it can be dramatically faster on lossy paths
but can also crowd out well-behaved Reno/CUBIC flows. Modern AQM (CoDel, FQ-CoDel) attacks the same
bufferbloat problem from the router side.
</Callout>

## ECN: congestion without dropping

**Explicit Congestion Notification** lets a router signal congestion by **marking** a bit in the IP
header instead of dropping the packet. The receiver echoes the CE mark back to the sender with the
**ECE** flag; the sender reduces cwnd as if it had seen a loss and sets **CWR** on its next segment to
signal it responded (only ECE travels receiver→sender) — but *no data was lost*, so there's no
retransmission and no latency spike. ECN needs support at both endpoints and the routers in between;
where deployed (notably data centers, and increasingly the public internet via L4S), it removes loss
as the price of congestion feedback.

<WorkedExample title="Why one loss cripples a long-fat pipe under Reno">
A 10 Gbit/s link with a 100 ms RTT has a bandwidth-delay product of `10×10⁹ × 0.1 / 8 ≈ 125 MB`
(~83,000 1500-byte segments) that must be in flight to stay full.

1. A single triple-dup-ACK loss halves cwnd — from ~83,000 to ~41,500 segments.
2. Reno climbs back at **+1 segment per RTT**. Recovering 41,500 segments at one per 100 ms RTT takes
   `41,500 × 0.1 s ≈ 69 minutes`.
3. During that climb the pipe is under-filled, so average throughput sits far below 10 Gbit/s.

This is exactly the gap **CUBIC** (cubic growth recovers the window in seconds, not minutes) and **BBR**
(paces to the measured BDP directly) were built to close.
</WorkedExample>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms rooted in congestion control">
- **Throughput far below link rate on a long-fat network** — Reno's slow recovery after loss; switch to
  CUBIC/BBR, and ensure window scaling is on (see [TCP internals](../tcp/)).
- **High latency under load (bufferbloat)** — loss-based CC filling a deep buffer; deploy AQM
  (FQ-CoDel) and/or ECN.
- **TCP incast collapse** — many senders hit one receiver (data-center map-reduce), synchronized RTOs
  crater goodput; mitigated by smaller RTO timers, ECN/DCTCP, and application-level staggering.
- **One flow starving others** — a BBR flow sharing a buffer with CUBIC, or a flow with a much shorter
  RTT winning the AIMD race (RTT unfairness).
</Callout>
