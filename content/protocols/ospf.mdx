## Link-state routing, industrial strength

OSPF is the dominant **interior gateway protocol** — the one routing packets inside enterprises,
campuses, and many ISP backbones (its main rival, IS-IS, runs the rest). Where BGP routes *between*
organizations on policy, OSPF routes *within* one on math: every router learns the complete
topology and independently computes shortest paths with **Dijkstra's algorithm**.

## How it works

**1. Hello and adjacency.** Routers multicast **Hello** packets (to 224.0.0.5) to discover
neighbors and verify two-way liveness. On multi-access networks (Ethernet), the segment elects a
**designated router** (DR) so n routers don't need n² adjacencies — everyone synchronizes with the
DR instead.

**2. Flood the map.** Each router describes its links and their **costs** in **link-state
advertisements** (LSAs), flooded reliably to every router in the area. Each LSA carries a sequence
number and age; routers store them all in an identical **link-state database** (LSDB). The
guarantee that matters: *every router has the same map*.

**3. Run Dijkstra.** Each router computes the shortest-path tree from itself over the LSDB and
installs the resulting next hops in its forwarding table. Cost is configurable per interface —
classically `reference-bandwidth / interface-bandwidth`, so a 10 Gb/s link is 10× cheaper than
1 Gb/s. Equal-cost paths are all installed (**ECMP**), spreading load.

**4. React to change.** A link dies → its router floods a new LSA → everyone re-runs Dijkstra.
Convergence is typically sub-second on modern gear — the decisive advantage over distance-vector's
rumor propagation and count-to-infinity pathologies.

<WorkedExample title="Why areas exist">
Flooding and Dijkstra scale with topology size — every change makes every router recompute. OSPF's
answer is hierarchy:

1. Split the network into **areas**; all inter-area traffic transits **area 0** (the backbone).
2. Routers see full topology only *within* their area; **area border routers** summarize other
   areas as plain prefixes (distance-vector-like at the boundary).
3. A flapping link in area 3 now triggers recomputation only in area 3 — the blast radius of
   instability is contained.

The design lesson generalizes: hierarchy is how link-state protocols buy scale, at the cost of
optimal paths across area boundaries.
</WorkedExample>

## Details that matter in practice

- **Runs directly on IP** (protocol 89) — no TCP/UDP. Reliability is built in via explicit LSA
  acknowledgments.
- **LSA types are the exam favorite:** type 1 (router links), type 2 (network/DR), type 3
  (inter-area summary), type 5 (external routes redistributed into OSPF, e.g. from BGP or statics).
- **Timers:** Hello every 10 s, dead after 40 s (4× missed) on broadcast networks — and both ends
  must agree or the adjacency never forms (a classic misconfiguration).
- **Passive interfaces:** advertise a subnet without speaking OSPF on it — standard hygiene on
  user-facing ports.
- **OSPFv3** carries IPv6 (and, with address families, IPv4) — same algorithm, reworked packet
  formats, link-local transport.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The classic failure modes">
Adjacencies stuck in *ExStart/Exchange* → MTU mismatch between neighbors. Adjacency never forms →
mismatched hello/dead timers, area IDs, or authentication. Suboptimal routing after a merger →
inconsistent reference bandwidth, so 10 Gb/s and 100 Gb/s links cost the same. Routing meltdown →
someone redistributed the full BGP table into OSPF (type-5 LSA flood) — the canonical "never do
this."
</Callout>

## Security

<Callout variant="security" title="Authenticate your IGP">
An attacker who can speak OSPF on your network can advertise themselves as the best path to
anything — interior MITM with one crafted LSA. Defense: cryptographic authentication on every
adjacency (MD5 historically; SHA via RFC 5709), passive interfaces everywhere users live, and
treating IGP participation as a privileged, switch-port-controlled capability.
</Callout>
