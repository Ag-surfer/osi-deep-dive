## Federation that actually federated

Email is the internet's oldest still-thriving decentralized application: anyone can run a server,
and mail flows between organizations with no central operator — 1982's architecture carrying
hundreds of billions of messages a day. SMTP is its delivery engine: a **store-and-forward push**
protocol where each hop takes custody, queues, and retries for days. (Reading mail is the pull
side — IMAP — and the division of labor is the first thing to get straight.)

## The conversation

SMTP is humane plaintext — you can speak it by hand:

```
220 mail.example.com ESMTP
EHLO sender.example.org
250-mail.example.com [capabilities: STARTTLS, SIZE, 8BITMIME…]
MAIL FROM:<alice@example.org>
250 OK
RCPT TO:<bob@example.com>
250 OK
DATA
354 Start mail input; end with <CRLF>.<CRLF>
From: Alice <alice@example.org>
Subject: Hello

The message body.
.
250 OK: queued as 4XyZ12
```

Two details there carry most of email's complexity. First, **the envelope is not the header**:
`MAIL FROM`/`RCPT TO` (the envelope, what servers route on) are independent of the `From:`/`To:`
header lines users see — mailing lists, BCC, and most spoofing all live in that gap. Second, the
**reply codes** (2xx accepted, 4xx temporary — retry later, 5xx permanent — bounce) drive the
queueing behavior that makes email resilient: a dead server means delay, not loss.

The journey: your client **submits** on port **587** (authenticated, TLS) to your provider's MTA;
that MTA looks up the recipient domain's **MX records** and relays on port **25** to theirs; the
recipient pulls it later via IMAP (993). Port 465 survives as implicit-TLS submission; port 25 is
inter-server only (ISPs block it outbound from consumer space precisely to choke spam-bot direct
delivery).

## MIME and the 7-bit inheritance

SMTP moves 7-bit ASCII lines. Everything else — attachments, HTML, emoji, non-Latin scripts —
rides **MIME**: typed body parts (`Content-Type: multipart/mixed`), Base64 transfer encoding
(hence attachments inflating ~33%), and encoded headers. A modern message is a MIME tree the
protocol itself never looks inside.

## The authentication retrofit

<Callout variant="security" title="SPF + DKIM + DMARC, in one breath each">
SMTP shipped with zero sender authentication — anyone could claim to be anyone, and for two
decades they did. The retrofit arrived as three DNS-published mechanisms: **SPF** — "these IPs may
send mail whose *envelope* sender is my domain" (breaks on forwarding, checks the wrong identity
for what users see). **DKIM** — the sending server cryptographically signs selected headers and
body; the public key sits in DNS; the signature survives relaying. **DMARC** — the keystone: ties
SPF/DKIM results to the *visible* `From:` domain (alignment), tells receivers what to do on
failure (`none`→`quarantine`→`reject`), and mails you aggregate reports of who's sending as you.
Deploy all three or watch your mail rot in spam folders: the big providers now effectively require
it.
</Callout>

Transport encryption is its own retrofit: **STARTTLS** upgrades the port-25 session
opportunistically, which a path attacker can strip; **MTA-STS** and **DANE** are the
policy mechanisms that make TLS-or-bust enforceable between providers.

<WorkedExample title="Why did this email land in spam? — the triage">
1. Open the headers and find `Authentication-Results:` at the receiving provider — three verdicts:
   `spf=`, `dkim=`, `dmarc=`.
2. `spf=fail` with a forwarder in `Received:` chain → classic SPF/forwarding breakage; DKIM should
   have saved it — check verdict two.
3. `dkim=fail` → the body or signed headers were modified in transit (mailing-list footers are
   notorious) or the selector's DNS key is wrong/rotated.
4. `dmarc=fail` with `p=reject` → receivers were *instructed* to refuse; check alignment: does the
   `From:` domain match what SPF/DKIM validated?
5. All three pass? Then it's reputation/content scoring — warm up the sending IP, fix the
   list-hygiene bounces, and stop writing like spam.
</WorkedExample>
