## The eight-byte philosophy

UDP adds exactly four things to IP — source port, destination port, length, checksum — in eight
bytes, and stops. No connection, no ordering, no retransmission, no congestion control. RFC 768 is
*three pages long*. That minimalism isn't a historical accident to be fixed; it's a deliberate
position: give applications **ports plus a checksum** and let them choose everything else.

## When "no promises" is the right promise

The cases where UDP wins share a shape — TCP's guarantees would cost more than they're worth:

- **A late packet is worse than a lost one.** Live voice/video and games can't use a retransmitted
  frame that arrives after its moment; better to conceal the loss and move on. TCP would stall the
  stream to deliver stale data in order.
- **The exchange is one round trip.** DNS: ~100 bytes each way. A TCP handshake would double the
  cost before the question is even asked.
- **You're broadcasting or multicasting.** Connections are point-to-point by definition; DHCP's
  DISCOVER and mDNS rely on UDP's connectionless sends.
- **You want to build your own transport.** The biggest modern reason — see below.

The price: the application owns retries, dedup, ordering, and — this one is an internet-citizenship
obligation (RFC 8085) — **congestion control**. An unthrottled UDP sender will happily bury a
network.

## Details engineers actually hit

- **"Connection refused" on UDP exists**: a datagram to a closed port triggers ICMP *Port
  Unreachable* — which is also how classic traceroute detects the final hop.
- **The checksum is optional in IPv4** (all-zeros = none) but **mandatory in IPv6**, since IPv6
  dropped its header checksum entirely.
- **Fragmentation is the silent killer.** A 9 KB UDP datagram becomes 7 IP fragments; lose any one
  and the whole datagram dies (and PMTUD black holes hit UDP with no TCP-style recovery). Serious
  UDP protocols keep datagrams under ~1,400 bytes — QUIC mandates ≥1,200-byte path support and
  probes upward.
- **NAT timeouts are brutal**: with no connection to observe, NATs expire UDP mappings in tens of
  seconds — why every UDP-based protocol (WireGuard, QUIC, VoIP) has keepalives.

<Callout variant="security" title="Spoofing and amplification">
No handshake means no proof the source address is real. Combine spoofable sources with services
whose replies dwarf their queries (old DNS, NTP `monlist`, memcached — up to 50,000×) and you get
**reflection/amplification DDoS**: attacker sends small spoofed queries "from" the victim; the
servers flood the victim with big answers. Defenses: BCP 38 source filtering at edges, rate
limiting and response-size limits on reflectors, and — for new protocols — handshakes that verify
return routability before sending big replies (exactly what QUIC's Retry does).
</Callout>

## UDP as a foundation

The modern pattern reframes UDP entirely: it's the **substrate for user-space transports**. QUIC
(HTTP/3), WireGuard, DTLS, RTP/WebRTC — each wants behavior TCP can't offer (streams without
head-of-line blocking, connection migration, datagram crypto) and ossified middleboxes won't pass
a new IP protocol number. UDP is the one hole through every NAT and firewall that lets innovation
ship. The kernel does ports; everything clever happens above.
