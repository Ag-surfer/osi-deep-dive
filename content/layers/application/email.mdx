## The mail pipeline

Email is a **store-and-forward** system: a message hops between independent servers, each taking
responsibility and queuing it until the next accepts it, so delivery survives outages and the sender
needn't wait for the recipient to be online. Three distinct jobs, on different protocols and ports:

- **Submission** — your mail client (**MUA**) hands the message to its outgoing server (**MSA/MTA**) on
  **port 587**, authenticated and over TLS.
- **Transfer** — the sender's server looks up the recipient domain's **MX record** in [DNS](../dns/) and
  relays the message to the recipient's server over **SMTP, port 25**, with the classic
  `HELO/EHLO → MAIL FROM → RCPT TO → DATA` conversation. There's an **envelope** (who to deliver to,
  used for routing) distinct from the visible **From:/To:** headers.
- **Retrieval** — the recipient's client fetches mail with **IMAP** (server-side, keeps mail in sync
  across devices) or the older **POP** (download, often delete).

## The spoofing problem and its fix

SMTP was designed in a trusting era: **nothing in the base protocol verifies the sender.** Any server can
write any address in `MAIL FROM` or the `From:` header — which is precisely why email is the universal
vector for **spam and phishing**. The modern defense is a trio of DNS-published policies that, together,
let a recipient verify a message really came from the domain it claims:

| Mechanism | What it checks | Published as |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **SPF** | Is the sending **server's IP** authorized to send for this domain? | DNS TXT record listing allowed senders |
| **DKIM** | Is the message **cryptographically signed** by the domain, and unmodified? | A signature header; public key in DNS |
| **DMARC** | Do SPF/DKIM **align with the visible `From:` domain**, and what to do if not? | DNS policy (none/quarantine/reject) + reporting |

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why all three are needed">
**SPF** alone breaks when mail is forwarded (the relaying IP changes) and only checks the envelope, not
the visible `From:`. **DKIM** survives forwarding (the signature travels with the message) but doesn't,
by itself, tie the signature to the `From:` a human sees. **DMARC** closes the loop: it requires that a
passing SPF *or* DKIM result **align** with the `From:` domain, tells receivers what to do on failure
(quarantine/reject), and sends the domain owner **reports** of who's sending as them. Together they turn
"anyone can claim to be you" into "only authorized senders can." Deploying them is now table stakes for
deliverability — without DMARC, your mail increasingly lands in spam.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms and the cause behind them">
- **Legitimate mail lands in spam** — missing or misaligned **SPF/DKIM/DMARC**; verify all three pass and
  align with the `From:` domain.
- **Delivery delayed by minutes** — **greylisting** (the receiver temporarily rejects unknown senders,
  expecting a well-behaved server to retry) or a backed-up queue.
- **Rejected as 'blocklisted'** — the sending IP's reputation is poor (shared host, prior abuse, or a
  compromised account sending spam).
- **Forwarded mail fails SPF** — expected; the relay's IP isn't in the original domain's SPF. DKIM and
  DMARC alignment are what carry forwarded mail.
</Callout>

## Security considerations

<Callout variant="security" title="Securing a 40-year-old protocol">
- **Spoofing/phishing** — the core weakness; **SPF + DKIM + DMARC** authenticate the sending domain, and
  **BIMI** adds a verified brand logo on top.
- **In-transit encryption** — **STARTTLS** opportunistically encrypts server-to-server SMTP, and
  **MTA-STS / DANE** prevent an attacker from stripping it back to plaintext.
- **Open relays** — a misconfigured MTA that relays mail for anyone becomes a spam cannon; never accept
  unauthenticated relay for external domains.
- **Account compromise** — a stolen mailbox sends *authenticated*, DMARC-passing spam; defended by MFA
  and anomaly detection, not by the email protocols themselves.
</Callout>
