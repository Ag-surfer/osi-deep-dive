## The protocol that holds the internet together

The internet is ~75,000 independently operated networks — **autonomous systems** (ASes), each with
a number (Google is AS15169, your ISP has one too). BGP is how they tell each other what addresses
they can reach. Every inter-provider link on earth runs it; when BGP breaks, companies vanish from
the internet (Facebook's 2021 six-hour outage was a BGP withdrawal). It is, famously, "the protocol
that holds the internet together with duct tape."

<Callout variant="insight" title="BGP optimizes for policy, not distance">
Interior protocols ask "what's the shortest path?" BGP asks "what's the path my business
relationships allow?" An ISP carries traffic for **customers** (they pay), exchanges it with
**peers** (free, mutual), and buys it from **providers**. Routes learned from customers are
preferred over peers over providers — *money first, hops later*. Understanding this single fact
explains most "weird" internet routing.
</Callout>

## How it works

BGP is almost insultingly simple mechanically — and bottomlessly deep operationally:

1. Two routers establish a **TCP connection on port 179** (BGP trusts TCP for reliability — a
   routing protocol riding on the transport layer).
2. They exchange **OPEN** messages (AS numbers, capabilities), then full routing tables once, then
   only **incremental UPDATEs**: "prefix 203.0.113.0/24 is reachable via this path" or a
   **withdrawal**. **KEEPALIVEs** (typically every 60 s) prove liveness; a **NOTIFICATION**
   tears the session down.
3. Each route carries **path attributes**, and the best-path algorithm compares them in strict
   order. The ones that matter daily:

| Attribute | What it does | Who it influences |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **LOCAL_PREF** | Operator's explicit preference — checked first | Outbound traffic (within your AS) |
| **AS_PATH** | Every AS the route has traversed | Loop prevention + shorter-is-better tiebreak |
| **MED** | "If you have several links to me, prefer this one" | Inbound traffic (a hint to neighbors) |
| **NEXT_HOP** | Where to actually forward | The data plane |
| **Communities** | Free-form tags ("customer route", "don't export to peers") | Policy automation |

<WorkedExample title="Loop prevention with no map and no TTL">
AS 64500 receives a route whose AS_PATH is `64510 64520 64500`.

1. It scans the path for its own AS number — and finds it.
2. The route is rejected unconditionally: accepting it would mean traffic could circle back
   through 64500 forever.
3. That's the entire loop-prevention mechanism: **the path itself is the loop detector** — no
   topology map (link-state), no hop counter (distance-vector). This is what "path-vector
   protocol" means.
</WorkedExample>

## The operational realities every engineer should know

- **Convergence is slow.** A withdrawn prefix can take minutes to settle globally as ASes explore
  alternate paths (path hunting). BGP favors stability over speed — **route flap damping** and
  **MRAI timers** deliberately slow churn.
- **The global table is huge.** ~1 million IPv4 prefixes. Every default-free router carries them
  all, which is why **route aggregation** and prefix-length filters (most ASes reject anything
  longer than /24) matter.
- **iBGP vs eBGP.** eBGP speaks between ASes; iBGP redistributes those routes *inside* an AS — and
  because iBGP doesn't append to AS_PATH, it can't detect loops the same way, demanding a full
  mesh, **route reflectors**, or confederations.
- **Traffic engineering is asymmetric.** You control where your traffic *leaves* (LOCAL_PREF) far
  more easily than where it *enters* (you can only hint — MED, AS_PATH prepending, or advertising
  more-specific prefixes).

## Security: the internet's softest underbelly

<Callout variant="security" title="Hijacks, leaks, and the slow fix">
Classic BGP believes whatever it's told. **Prefix hijacking** — announcing address space you don't
own — has rerouted YouTube through Pakistan (2008) and cryptocurrency traffic through attackers
(repeatedly). **Route leaks** — re-advertising routes you shouldn't (a customer announcing its two
providers to each other) — have melted national connectivity by accident. The fixes arriving over
the last decade: **RPKI route-origin validation** (cryptographic proof that an AS may originate a
prefix — now covering the majority of routes), AS_PATH filtering, and the **MANRS** operator
norms. Full path validation (BGPsec) exists on paper but not, meaningfully, on the internet.
</Callout>
