## The contract

TCP's promise is audacious: a **reliable, ordered, error-checked byte stream** between two
processes, built on a network that loses, duplicates, reorders, and delays packets. Five decades
on, it still carries most of the internet — every HTTPS page (until HTTP/3), every SSH session,
every database connection. Understanding TCP deeply is the closest thing networking has to a
universal skill.

The stream abstraction is the part beginners miss: TCP has **no message boundaries**. You write
100 bytes twice; the receiver may read 200 once, or 50 four times. Every protocol above TCP must
frame its own messages (HTTP's Content-Length, length-prefixed records) — forgetting this is the
root of a thousand parsing bugs.

## The header, field by field

<NamedHeader id="tcp" />

The fields tell the whole design: ports (multiplexing), sequence/acknowledgment numbers
(reliability and ordering), window (flow control), flags (connection state machine), checksum
(integrity, computed with the pseudo-header), options (the extension mechanism that kept a 1981
protocol modern).

## Connection lifecycle

**Setup — three-way handshake.** SYN → SYN-ACK → ACK synchronizes both directions' randomized
initial sequence numbers and negotiates options. The critical options are negotiated only on SYNs: **MSS**
(biggest segment I accept), **window scale** (multiply the 16-bit window by up to 2¹⁴ — without it
throughput dies on high-BDP paths), **SACK-permitted**, and **timestamps** (negotiated on the SYN,
then carried on every segment for RTT measurement + wrapped-sequence protection).

**Teardown — four messages, two half-closes.** Each side independently FINs its direction; data
can still flow the other way (half-close). The side that closes first lands in **TIME_WAIT** for
2×MSL — to retransmit the last ACK if needed and to let old duplicates die before the port pair is
reused. The state machine in between (LISTEN, SYN_SENT, ESTABLISHED, FIN_WAIT, CLOSE_WAIT…) is
what `ss`/`netstat` show you; piles of CLOSE_WAIT mean *your application* forgot to close sockets.

## Reliability machinery

- **Cumulative ACKs**: "I have every byte before N." Cheap, robust, but says nothing about later
  data that arrived.
- **SACK** fills that gap: "…and also ranges N+1000–N+2000," enabling selective retransmission
  instead of resending everything.
- **Retransmission timeout**: continuously estimated as `SRTT + 4·RTTVAR` (RFC 6298), doubled on
  every consecutive failure; **Karn's rule** excludes retransmitted segments from the estimate.
- **Fast retransmit**: three duplicate ACKs imply a specific loss — resend immediately, no timeout
  wait.

## The two throttles

**Flow control** protects the receiver: the advertised window is the buffer space left; a window
of zero pauses the sender (probed by keepalive-like *window probes* — and shrunken-window bugs are
why the **silly window syndrome** avoidance rules exist). **Congestion control** protects the
network: slow start's exponential growth to a threshold, then AIMD — with the modern story (CUBIC,
BBR) covered in depth on the [Transport layer page](/layers/transport/). Effective rate is always
`min(rwnd, cwnd)` per RTT.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Numbers to carry around">
Throughput ≈ window / RTT. A 64 KB un-scaled window across 100 ms = ~5 Mb/s, on any link.
Mathis's loss formula — rate ∝ MSS / (RTT · √loss) — explains why fat links with tiny loss rates
still crawl, and why CDNs fight for RTT, not bandwidth.
</Callout>

## Operational gotchas that fill on-call retros

- **Nagle's algorithm vs delayed ACKs**: both batch tiny packets to save overhead; combined, they
  can add 40–200 ms stalls to chatty request/response patterns — why latency-sensitive software
  sets `TCP_NODELAY`.
- **Keepalives are off by default** and hours-long when on; middleboxes drop idle state in
  minutes. Long-lived idle connections need application-level heartbeats.
- **SYN floods** exhaust the half-open table; **SYN cookies** encode the connection state into the
  ISN itself so the server stores nothing until the handshake completes.
- **RST semantics**: instant "refused" = reachable host, no listener (or a REJECT firewall);
  silent timeout = packets vanishing. Reading the failure mode is free diagnosis.
