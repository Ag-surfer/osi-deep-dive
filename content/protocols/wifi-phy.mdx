## Making radio behave like a cable

Turning open-air radio into a 10-gigabit LAN link is a far harder physics problem than driving a
wire, and the 802.11 physical layer is the accumulated answer. This page is the radio underneath
the [Wireless deep dive](/wireless/) and the [Data Link page's CSMA/CA](/layers/data-link/): how
bits become radio waves robust enough to survive walls, microwaves, and a hundred neighbors'
networks.

## Bands and channels: the spectrum you're fighting over

| Band | Character | The trade-off |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **2.4 GHz** | Long range, penetrates walls | Crowded (Bluetooth, microwaves, old gear); only **3 non-overlapping** 20 MHz channels — congestion central |
| **5 GHz** | More channels, faster | Shorter range, weaker through walls; some channels share with radar (DFS) |
| **6 GHz** (Wi-Fi 6E/7) | Clean, wide, lots of channels | Newest devices only; even shorter range |

Channel **width** is a core lever: bonding 20 MHz channels into 40/80/160/320 MHz multiplies
throughput — but a wide channel is harder to find clear, more prone to interference, and (in
crowded 2.4 GHz) antisocial. The eternal Wi-Fi tension: width for speed vs narrow for coexistence.

## OFDM: hundreds of slow streams beat one fast one

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why Wi-Fi splits the channel into subcarriers">
A single fast signal is wrecked by **multipath** — in a room, the signal arrives many times over,
bounced off walls at different delays, and the echoes smear fast symbols into each other. **OFDM**
(orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing) splits the channel into hundreds of narrow
**subcarriers**, each carrying a *slow* stream whose symbols are long enough to ride out the
echoes (helped by a **guard interval** between symbols). Hundreds of slow parallel streams are
dramatically more multipath-robust than one fast one — and if interference clobbers a few
subcarriers, only those bits are lost, not the whole link. OFDM is the foundation that made
high-rate indoor Wi-Fi (and LTE/5G, and cable) possible.
</Callout>

## The speed levers, generation by generation

The same three knobs as [Ethernet PHY](/protocols/ethernet-phy/), pulled in the air:

1. **Modulation density (QAM)**: each subcarrier carries a constellation point. 64-QAM = 6 bits,
   256-QAM (Wi-Fi 5) = 8, 1024-QAM (Wi-Fi 6) = 10, 4096-QAM (Wi-Fi 7) = 12 — and each denser step
   needs higher SNR, which is why your link rate *drops as you walk away*: **adaptive modulation**
   trades bits/symbol for robustness as signal fades.
2. **Channel width**: 20 → 40 → 80 → 160 → 320 MHz.
3. **Spatial streams (MIMO)**: multiple antennas send *independent* data streams on the same
   frequency, separated by the math of how they bounce differently to the receiver — 4×4 MIMO ≈ 4×
   the data. **MU-MIMO** serves several clients at once; **beamforming** shapes the antenna pattern
   toward a specific client for more SNR.

Stack all three and Wi-Fi 7's headline numbers appear — but the *real-world* rate is always
gated by SNR, interference, and how many neighbors are talking.

## OFDMA: scheduling arrives in Wi-Fi

Wi-Fi 6's signature change borrows from cellular. Classic Wi-Fi gives a transmitting station the
*whole* channel for its turn — wasteful for the small, frequent packets of IoT and chatty apps.
**OFDMA** subdivides the channel into **resource units** and assigns different chunks to different
clients *simultaneously*, so the AP packs many small transmissions into one airtime slot. It's a
shift from pure contention toward AP **scheduling** — the same philosophical move that separates
[Wi-Fi from cellular](/wireless/), creeping into Wi-Fi itself.

## Why it never matches the box's number

<Callout variant="warning" title="Rated vs real">
The gigabit figure on the router box is a *PHY* maximum: peak QAM, max width, all spatial streams,
ideal SNR, one client. Reality subtracts relentlessly — MAC overhead and the CSMA/CA
listen-before-talk tax (~half the airtime can be protocol, not payload), interference forcing
lower QAM, walls cutting SNR, and every other device *sharing the same airtime*. Wi-Fi is a
**shared half-duplex medium**: total capacity is divided among everyone talking, and one slow
distant client drags the whole cell down by hogging airtime at a low rate. Expecting wire-like,
per-device, full-duplex throughput is the root of most "my Wi-Fi is slow" disappointment.
</Callout>
