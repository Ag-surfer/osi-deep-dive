## Same frame, a thousandfold faster

The Ethernet *frame* barely changed from 1980 to today; the **physical layer underneath it** was
rebuilt over and over to go from 10 Mb/s on a shared coax bus to **800 Gb/s** on parallel fiber —
a five-order-of-magnitude climb achieved by pulling the same handful of Layer-1 levers harder each
generation. This page is about those levers: line coding, modulation, and lanes.

## The naming code, decoded

`1000BASE-T` looks cryptic and is actually a precise spec: **speed–signaling–medium**.

| Name | Speed | Medium | The Layer-1 story |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 10BASE-T | 10 Mb/s | 2 twisted pairs | Manchester coding — simple, self-clocking, 2× bandwidth cost |
| 100BASE-TX | 100 Mb/s | 2 pairs | 4B/5B + MLT-3 — denser coding to hit 100M on the same cable |
| **1000BASE-T** | 1 Gb/s | **4 pairs** | All four pairs at once, **PAM-5**, echo cancellation, bidirectional per pair |
| 10GBASE-T | 10 Gb/s | 4 pairs (Cat 6a) | PAM-16 + heavy DSP; runs hot, why fiber often wins past 10G |
| 100GBASE-SR4 | 100 Gb/s | parallel fiber | 4 lanes × 25G; lanes are how you scale past one stream's limit |

The `T` = twisted pair, `S`/`L` = short/long fiber, the trailing number = lane count. Decoding the
name tells you the cabling, reach, and roughly the era.

## The levers, and why they have ceilings

Three knobs raise the bitrate, each bounded by [Nyquist and Shannon](/layers/physical/):

1. **Symbol rate (baud)** — more symbols per second, capped by the channel's bandwidth and how
   fast the electronics can cleanly switch.
2. **Bits per symbol (modulation density)** — PAM-5, PAM-16: more voltage levels per symbol, but
   the levels crowd together and need ever-better signal-to-noise to tell apart. This is why
   10GBASE-T over copper demands Cat 6a and burns power on DSP — it's fighting noise to keep the
   levels distinct.
3. **Parallel lanes** — when one stream hits its ceiling, run several. 1000BASE-T uses all four
   copper pairs; 100G/400G fiber uses multiple wavelengths or fibers. Most modern speed jumps are
   really lane-count jumps.

<WorkedExample title="Why gigabit copper needs all four pairs (and why a damaged one drops you to 100M)">
1. 100BASE-TX moved 100 Mb/s over **2** pairs.
2. 1000BASE-T needed 10× that on the same Cat 5e. Rather than one impossibly fast pair, it spreads
   250 Mb/s across **4** pairs simultaneously — and transmits in *both directions on each pair at
   once* (hybrid + echo cancellation), a DSP feat.
3. So if a re-termination breaks one pair, gigabit can't form — autonegotiation falls back to
   100BASE-TX, which only needs two good pairs. The classic "why did this link come up at 100?"
   ticket is a dead pair, and it's a direct consequence of the lane math.
</WorkedExample>

## The features that ship with the PHY

- **Autonegotiation**: link partners exchange capability pulses and settle on the best common
  speed/duplex — the source of the canonical **duplex-mismatch** misery when one side is hard-coded
  (forced full-duplex vs auto → the auto side picks half → late collisions, ~working-but-awful
  throughput).
- **Power over Ethernet (802.3af/at/bt)**: up to ~90 W of DC riding the same cable as data — APs,
  cameras, and phones powered by their network drop. A Layer-1 capability with outsized real-world
  reach.
- **Auto-MDI-X**: the PHY swaps transmit/receive pairs itself, which is why crossover cables
  vanished.
- **SFP/QSFP transceivers**: the PHY became *modular* — swap a copper, short-fiber, or long-fiber
  optic into the same cage, decoupling the switch from the medium.

<Callout variant="insight" title="The pattern across 40 years">
Every Ethernet generation is the same three levers — faster symbols, denser modulation, more lanes
— plus more DSP to claw signal out of noise. The frame on top never noticed. It's the cleanest
illustration of *why* the OSI model splits Layer 1 from Layer 2: the link's job ('deliver this
frame') stayed constant while its physics were reinvented underneath, and the layer boundary is
exactly what let that happen.
</Callout>
