## The glass the internet runs on

Essentially all long-haul internet traffic — between cities, under oceans, across continents —
travels as pulses of light in **optical fiber**, a strand of ultra-pure glass thinner than a hair.
Fiber wins for three physical reasons copper can't touch: **enormous bandwidth** (light oscillates
at ~200 THz, so there's vast spectrum to modulate), **immunity to electromagnetic interference**
(it's glass — no antenna effect, no crosstalk), and **astonishingly low loss** (a signal can run
~80 km before needing amplification). The submarine cables crossing the Pacific are this, bundled
and armored.

## Single-mode vs multi-mode: the one distinction to know

<Callout variant="insight" title="It's about the light's path, not the data rate">
**Multi-mode** fiber has a wide core (~50 µm) in which light bounces along *many* paths ("modes").
Cheap LEDs/VCSELs drive it, but the modes arrive at slightly different times — **modal
dispersion** smears the pulse — so it's limited to short reach (data-center rows, building risers,
~hundreds of meters). **Single-mode** fiber has a core so narrow (~9 µm) that light travels
essentially **one path**, eliminating modal dispersion; driven by precise lasers, it reaches tens
to thousands of kilometers. The rule of thumb: **multi-mode inside a building, single-mode between
buildings and cities.** Mixing them, or mismatching transceiver to fiber type, is a common and
baffling field fault.
</Callout>

## DWDM: many colors, one fiber

A single laser uses a tiny slice of the fiber's capacity. **Wavelength-division multiplexing**
runs *many* independent signals down one strand, each on its own **wavelength** (color) of light —
they don't interfere any more than red and blue light do, and a prism-like device combines them in
and splits them out. **Dense WDM** (ITU G.694.1) packs ~80–96 channels onto a precise frequency
grid (50 GHz spacing), each carrying 100–400 Gb/s+, for **tens of terabits per second on a single
fiber pair**. It's frequency-division multiplexing — the same idea as radio stations sharing the
air — done with light, and it's why a fiber laid decades ago keeps getting faster: you upgrade the
*optics on the ends*, not the buried glass.

<WorkedExample title="Why a 20-year-old fiber keeps getting faster">
1. The glass between two cities was laid once and is essentially passive — it just guides light.
2. Capacity lives in the **transceivers** at each end: a 2005 link might have run one 10G
   wavelength; swap in modern DWDM gear and the *same fiber* carries 80 wavelengths × 400G.
3. The lever is **coherent optics** — modern transceivers modulate light's amplitude *and phase*
   (QAM on a light wave) and use DSP to undo dispersion electronically, squeezing far more bits
   per wavelength than on-off keying ever could.

So the capacity curve climbs without digging: investment moves to the endpoints, and the buried
glass is a decades-long asset. This is the economics that built the internet's backbone.
</WorkedExample>

## The supporting cast

- **EDFAs** (erbium-doped fiber amplifiers): amplify *all* DWDM wavelengths at once, *optically*,
  without converting back to electronics — the invention that made long-haul WDM economic.
- **Loss budget**: every connector, splice, and kilometer costs decibels; designing a link means
  adding up the losses and confirming the receiver still hears the signal. The everyday version of
  Shannon's SNR limit.
- **PONs** (passive optical networks): the fiber-to-the-home architecture — one fiber from the
  exchange split passively among ~32 homes, each on a time/wavelength slot. The "fiber broadband"
  on your bill.

<Callout variant="security" title="Tapping the untappable">
Fiber feels secure — no radiated signal to catch from afar — but a determined attacker can bend a
fiber and capture the trace amount of light that leaks (microbends), or splice in a coupler. State-
level submarine-cable tapping is documented. The lesson is the universal one: **assume the medium
is hostile and encrypt above it.** Optical-layer encryption exists for backbone links, but
end-to-end TLS is what actually protects your data regardless of who's listening to the glass.
</Callout>
