## From baseband to a carrier

[Line coding](../signal-encoding/) drives a signal straight onto a wire near DC — fine for copper, but
useless on a channel that only passes a band of frequencies far from zero: radio, DSL, cable, optical
fiber. There you **modulate a carrier** — vary some property of a steady sine wave to carry the bits:

- **ASK** (amplitude-shift keying) — vary the carrier's amplitude.
- **FSK** (frequency-shift keying) — vary its frequency.
- **PSK** (phase-shift keying) — vary its phase (BPSK = 2 phases, QPSK = 4).

<Callout variant="insight" title="QAM: carry more bits per symbol">
**Quadrature Amplitude Modulation** combines amplitude *and* phase, placing symbols at points on a 2-D
**constellation**. With *M* points, each symbol carries **log₂(M) bits**: 16-QAM = 4 bits/symbol,
64-QAM = 6, 256-QAM = 8, and Wi-Fi 6's **1024-QAM** = 10. The catch: more points are packed closer
together, so it takes a **higher SNR** to tell them apart. Cram too many in and noise makes neighbors
indistinguishable — the bit error rate explodes. Every step up the QAM ladder buys throughput by
spending noise margin.
</Callout>

## Baud vs. bit rate

These are routinely confused. The **baud rate** (symbol rate) is how many symbols per second; the **bit
rate** is `baud × bits-per-symbol`. A 1,000-baud link using 256-QAM (8 bits/symbol) carries 8,000 bits/s.
This is why "9,600 baud" and "9,600 bps" are only equal for 2-level signaling — the moment you use a
richer constellation they diverge.

## OFDM: many slow carriers beat one fast one

Pushing symbols faster through a real channel makes them smear into each other (**inter-symbol
interference** from multipath echoes). **Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing** sidesteps this by
splitting the band into **hundreds of narrow, orthogonal subcarriers**, each carrying a slow QAM stream
in parallel. Slow symbols tolerate echo; orthogonality lets the subcarriers overlap without interfering;
and a **cyclic prefix** absorbs the remaining multipath. OFDM is why Wi-Fi, LTE/5G, DSL, and DOCSIS all
work over hostile channels — and it pairs naturally with **adaptive modulation**, dialing each
subcarrier's QAM up or down to the SNR it actually sees.

## The two hard limits

No modulation scheme escapes two theorems from the 1920s–40s:

- **Nyquist (noiseless):** the maximum symbol rate over a channel of bandwidth *B* is **2B**. With *M*
  levels, the bit rate ceiling is `2B · log₂(M)`. Nyquist answers *how many symbols per second* the
  bandwidth allows — and says you can always send more bits by adding levels…
- **Shannon (noisy):** …but noise caps it. The capacity of a channel with bandwidth *B* and
  signal-to-noise ratio *SNR* is **C = B · log₂(1 + SNR)** bits/s — an absolute ceiling **no** code or
  modulation can beat. Nyquist says "more levels = more bits"; Shannon says "only until noise drowns
  them."

<WorkedExample title="Why dial-up modems topped out near 33.6 kbps">
A telephone channel offers roughly **B = 3,100 Hz** of usable bandwidth at an SNR of about **30 dB**.

1. Convert SNR from dB to a ratio: `30 dB → 10^(30/10) = 1000`.
2. Shannon capacity: `C = 3100 × log₂(1 + 1000) = 3100 × log₂(1001) ≈ 3100 × 9.97 ≈ 30,900 bits/s`.
3. So **~31 kbps is the hard ceiling** of an analog phone line — which is exactly why V.34 modems
   maxed out at 33.6 kbps. (56k modems only beat it by being *digital* at the ISP end, removing one
   analog-to-digital conversion and its quantization noise — changing the channel, not the math.)
4. On the symbol side: the phone channel is **passband** (~300–3,400 Hz), and a carrier-modulated
   passband channel of bandwidth *B* carries about *B* symbols/s (QAM packs two dimensions, I and Q, per
   symbol) — so V.34 actually ran at a **3,429-baud** symbol rate. Reaching ~33.6 kbps over it therefore
   needs ≈ **10 bits/symbol**, which V.34 delivered with a large **trellis-coded QAM constellation
   (~1,664 points)**. Shannon's `B·log₂(1+SNR)` caps the *bit rate* (~31–34 kbps); the constellation
   size plus coding is how the modem chased that ceiling.
</WorkedExample>

<Callout variant="note" title="The whole game in one sentence">
You get more bits by using more bandwidth or a denser constellation; Shannon's `B·log₂(1+SNR)` says how
far each can take you before noise wins. Real systems (Wi-Fi, 5G, DOCSIS) chase that bound with
**adaptive modulation and coding** plus strong [forward error correction](../../data-link/error-detection/),
backing off the constellation as the SNR drops.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms rooted in modulation & noise">
- **Throughput drops as you move away from the access point** — falling SNR forces a lower-order
  modulation (256-QAM → 64-QAM → … → BPSK); the link rate steps down to stay below the error cliff.
- **High error rate at a high data rate, fine at a lower one** — the chosen constellation is too dense
  for the channel's SNR; the receiver can't separate adjacent symbols.
- **Severe degradation indoors / in motion** — multipath fading and Doppler; OFDM with a cyclic prefix
  and robust coding is the mitigation.
- **A link that simply won't go faster despite a clean signal** — you've hit the Shannon ceiling for
  that bandwidth and SNR; the only levers left are more spectrum or a better SNR.
</Callout>
