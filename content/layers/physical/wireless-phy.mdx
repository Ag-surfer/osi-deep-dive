## Why the air is the hardest medium

A wire is a private, stable channel; the air is a **shared, hostile, time-varying** one. A wireless PHY
fights problems no cable has:

- **Shared spectrum** — everyone on a band contends for the same Hz, much of it unlicensed and crowded.
- **Multipath fading** — the signal arrives via several reflected paths that add up constructively or
  destructively, so received strength swings wildly with tiny movements.
- **Attenuation & interference** — distance, walls, and other emitters erode the SNR that
  [Shannon's limit](../modulation-capacity/) depends on.
- **Mobility / Doppler** — motion shifts frequencies and changes the channel continuously.
- **Half-duplex** — a radio can't usually transmit and receive on the same channel at once.

Everything below is a technique for wringing reliable bits out of that mess.

## Spectrum and channels

Wi-Fi lives in unlicensed bands with very different characteristics: **2.4 GHz** travels far and
penetrates walls but is crowded and has only **three non-overlapping 20 MHz channels**; **5 GHz** offers
many more channels and width at shorter range; **6 GHz** (Wi-Fi 6E/7) adds a large, clean swath.
Cellular instead uses **licensed** bands an operator owns exclusively — paying for spectrum to guarantee
interference-free service. Wider channels (20 → 40 → 80 → 160 → 320 MHz) carry more bits, but there are
fewer of them, so width trades capacity against the ability to avoid neighbors.

## Spread spectrum

Early Wi-Fi (and GPS, and 3G) deliberately **spread** each bit across far more bandwidth than it needs:

- **DSSS** (Direct-Sequence) multiplies each data bit by a faster **chipping code**. The receiver, which
  knows the code, correlates to recover the bit — gaining **processing gain** that pulls the signal out
  from under noise and narrowband interference, and lets multiple coded users share one band (the basis
  of **CDMA** in 3G).
- **FHSS** (Frequency-Hopping) rapidly hops the carrier across many frequencies in a known pattern, so a
  jammer or interferer on any one frequency only clips a fraction of the transmission (used by classic
  Bluetooth).

Spreading buys **robustness and sharing** at the cost of raw spectral efficiency — which is why modern
high-rate Wi-Fi moved to OFDM instead.

## OFDM and OFDMA

[OFDM](../modulation-capacity/) splits the channel into hundreds of narrow, orthogonal subcarriers, each
a slow QAM stream — the key to surviving multipath, and the foundation of Wi-Fi 5+, LTE, and 5G.
**OFDMA** (Wi-Fi 6 / LTE-A) goes further: it slices those subcarriers into **resource units** and hands
*different groups to different users simultaneously*. Instead of one device owning the whole channel per
turn, many share it at once — slashing latency and overhead for the dense, many-small-device traffic of
modern networks.

## MIMO and beamforming

Multiple antennas turn multipath from an enemy into an asset:

- **Spatial multiplexing (MIMO)** — send **independent data streams** from multiple antennas at once;
  the receiver's multiple antennas, seeing different mixes of the streams, solve them apart. *N* streams
  ≈ *N*× the throughput, the headline rate-multiplier of modern Wi-Fi.
- **Diversity** — send the *same* data over several paths so a fade on one doesn't kill the link
  (reliability instead of speed).
- **MU-MIMO** — serve **several users** their own streams simultaneously from one access point.
- **Beamforming** — phase the antennas so the signal **adds up in the direction of the client**,
  improving SNR and range. **Massive MIMO** (5G) scales this to dozens of antennas for tight,
  steerable beams.

<Callout variant="insight" title="The radio adapts every transmission to the channel">
There's no single "Wi-Fi speed." The radio constantly picks a **Modulation and Coding Set (MCS)** —
a QAM order (BPSK … 1024-QAM) plus a [forward-error-correction](../../data-link/error-detection/) rate —
matched to the SNR it currently measures. Strong signal → dense QAM, light coding, high rate; weak signal
→ rugged BPSK, heavy coding, low rate but still connected. Combined with channel width and spatial
streams, that's why the *same* link reports wildly different rates as you move around.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="history" title="The generations, in one line each">
**Wi-Fi:** 4 (802.11n, MIMO+40 MHz) → 5 (ac, wider 5 GHz, MU-MIMO) → 6/6E (ax, OFDMA + 6 GHz) → 7 (be,
320 MHz, 4096-QAM, multi-link). **Cellular:** 3G (CDMA spread spectrum) → 4G LTE (OFDMA) → 5G NR (massive
MIMO, mmWave, flexible numerology). Each leap is mostly a **PHY** story: more spectrum, denser
modulation, and more antennas.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms rooted in the wireless PHY">
- **Great signal bars, terrible throughput** — co-channel interference/contention: many radios sharing
  one channel. Pick a cleaner channel, narrow the width, or move to 5/6 GHz.
- **Throughput collapses as you walk away** — falling SNR forcing a lower MCS (1024-QAM → … → BPSK); the
  link rate steps down to stay error-free.
- **Erratic performance when standing still near metal/glass** — multipath fading nulls; MIMO/diversity
  and small position changes help.
- **2.4 GHz unusable in a dense area** — only three non-overlapping channels, all saturated; the band is
  full, not broken.
</Callout>
