## Recovering the clock from the data

A serial link has no separate clock wire — the receiver must figure out *where each bit is* from the
signal itself. It does this with a **Clock and Data Recovery (CDR)** circuit: a **phase-locked loop
(PLL)** that locks onto the signal's transitions and free-wheels through the stretches in between,
generating a local clock aligned to the sender's. This is exactly why [line coding](../signal-encoding/)
works so hard to guarantee transitions — a CDR starved of transitions (a long flat run) drifts out of
lock and starts misreading bits.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Jitter vs. wander">
Real edges don't arrive exactly on the ideal clock grid — they wobble. **Jitter** is the *fast* timing
variation (above ~10 Hz): from noise, inter-symbol interference, crosstalk, and supply ripple. **Wander**
is the *slow* drift (below ~10 Hz): from temperature and aging of reference oscillators. A CDR's PLL
tracks slow wander but must *tolerate* fast jitter within its margin — and that margin is exactly what an
eye diagram measures.
</Callout>

## The eye diagram

Overlay every short slice of the received signal, aligned to the recovered clock, and all the bit
patterns pile on top of each other. The clear opening in the middle — the **eye** — is the margin the
receiver has to decide 1 vs. 0. Its **height** is the **voltage (noise) margin**; its **width** is the
**timing (jitter) margin**. Watch it close as impairments grow:

A wide-open eye means easy, error-free sampling at the center. As noise spreads the levels and jitter
spreads the edges, the eye shrinks from both directions; when it closes, the sampler can no longer
separate ones from zeros and the **bit error rate** climbs. The eye diagram is the single most useful
picture of a link's physical health.

## Independent clocks drift: plesiochronous vs. synchronous

Two devices with their own free-running oscillators are **plesiochronous** — "almost synchronous." Even
high-quality clocks differ by a few parts per million, so over time the receiver reads slightly more or
fewer bits than the sender wrote: a **bit slip**. Networks handle this two ways:

- **Elastic buffers + justification** — a small FIFO absorbs the difference; when it drifts too full or
  empty, the system inserts or deletes a stuffing bit. **SONET/SDH** does this with **pointer
  adjustments** that let a payload "float" within the frame as clocks differ.
- **Synchronous networks** — distribute one **common timing reference** to everyone (a BITS clock
  traceable to an atomic source), so there's no relative drift to absorb. The traditional telecom
  approach.

<Callout variant="note" title="Modern timing: SyncE and PTP">
Packet networks broke the old synchronous TDM timing chain, so two technologies restore it for
applications that need precise time — cellular handoff, financial timestamping, power-grid telemetry:
- **Synchronous Ethernet (SyncE)** recovers a traceable **frequency** from the Ethernet PHY's line
  clock (a physical-layer mechanism, unaffected by packet load).
- **PTP / IEEE 1588** distributes **phase and time-of-day** to sub-microsecond accuracy using
  hardware timestamps applied **at the PHY** (so software/queuing jitter doesn't corrupt them).
Both beat **NTP** — a software, Application-layer protocol good to milliseconds — precisely because they
push timing down to the hardware.
</Callout>

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms rooted in timing & signal integrity">
- **Bursty errors on certain data patterns** — a marginal eye: ISI or jitter closing it for the
  worst-case bit sequences. Check the eye/signal-integrity, cabling, and transceiver.
- **Loss of lock / link flaps** — the CDR can't hold the clock: too few transitions (encoding/scrambler
  fault), excessive jitter, or a failing oscillator.
- **Periodic bit slips on a long-haul circuit** — plesiochronous clock mismatch overrunning the elastic
  buffer; verify the synchronization (clock source/traceability).
- **PTP time error spikes under load** — timestamps taken in software instead of at the PHY, or a
  non-PTP-aware switch in the path adding variable delay.
</Callout>
