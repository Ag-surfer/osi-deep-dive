## Why the link layer checks for errors

The [Physical layer](../../physical/) delivers bits, but noise, attenuation, and interference flip some
of them. The [Data Link layer](../../data-link/) adds a **Frame Check Sequence (FCS)** so a receiver can
**detect** a corrupted frame and discard it — recovery is left to higher layers (or to
[ARQ](../arq-framing/)). The dominant tool is the **Cyclic Redundancy Check**, and it is dominant for a
reason: it is cheap in hardware (just shifts and XORs) and mathematically strong against the **burst
errors** that real channels actually produce.

<Callout variant="insight" title="Detection, not correction — and that's deliberate">
Ethernet's CRC only *detects* errors; a frame that fails the check is silently dropped. Adding enough
redundancy to *correct* errors (forward error correction) costs bandwidth on every frame, which is
wasteful on a low-error wired link where a retransmission is rare and cheap. FEC earns its keep where
retransmission is expensive or impossible — deep-space links, Wi-Fi/5G radios, storage, optical — so it
lives mostly at the Physical layer there, not in the Ethernet FCS.
</Callout>

## CRC: error detection as polynomial division

A CRC treats the message as the coefficients of a binary polynomial and **divides** it by a fixed
**generator polynomial**, using XOR arithmetic (no carries). The **remainder** is the CRC. The sender
appends it; the receiver divides the whole frame (message + CRC) by the same generator and expects a
**zero remainder** — anything else means the frame was corrupted. Ethernet uses a specific 33-bit
generator to produce **CRC-32**.

<Callout variant="note" title="Why CRC is strong against burst errors">
A well-chosen generator polynomial of degree *r* guarantees detection of: **all single-bit errors**,
**all double-bit errors**, **any odd number of bit errors** (if the polynomial has the factor `x+1`),
and **every burst error of length *r* bits or shorter**. CRC-32's 32-bit remainder makes the odds that a random
corruption happens to produce a zero remainder about **1 in 4 billion**. That combination — burst
robustness plus a vanishingly small miss rate, in pure shift-and-XOR hardware — is why CRC won.
</Callout>

## The Internet checksum: weaker, but cheap in software

IP, TCP, and UDP use a much simpler **one's-complement sum** of 16-bit words (<RFCRef n={1071} />). It's
trivial to compute in software and easy to update incrementally (handy when a router rewrites a TTL),
but it's **far weaker** than a CRC — it misses reordered words and certain compensating errors. That's an
accepted trade: it's a lightweight end-to-end backstop above a link layer whose CRC already caught the
hard cases. (Notably, IPv6 dropped the IP-header checksum entirely, leaning on the link CRC and the
transport checksum.)

## Forward error correction in one breath

Where you *can't* just retransmit, you add enough structured redundancy to **reconstruct** the original.
**Hamming codes** (1950) add parity bits positioned so the syndrome of failed checks points directly at
the bit to flip — single-error correction. Modern radios and storage use far stronger codes
(Reed–Solomon, LDPC, turbo codes). The trade is always the same: FEC spends bandwidth on every block to
avoid a round-trip, while detection-plus-retransmission spends nothing until an error actually occurs.

## Failure modes & troubleshooting

<Callout variant="warning" title="Symptoms and the cause behind them">
- **Rising FCS / CRC error counters on an interface** — physical-layer trouble: a bad cable, failing
  transceiver/SFP, EMI, or a **duplex mismatch** (late collisions look like CRC errors). Check the
  cabling and matched duplex first.
- **Runts/giants with CRC errors** — frames truncated or oversized by a faulty NIC or driver.
- **Good link, occasional CRC drops under load** — marginal signal integrity; reseat/replace optics or
  shorten the run.
- **Errors that the CRC can't catch** show up as application-layer corruption — exactly why TCP keeps
  its own (weaker) checksum as a second line of defense.
</Callout>

## Security considerations

<Callout variant="security" title="A CRC is integrity, not authenticity">
A CRC detects **accidental** corruption, not **malicious** tampering: an attacker who changes the
payload can simply recompute the CRC, and the change passes undetected. Integrity against an adversary
requires a keyed construction — a **MAC** (HMAC) or an **AEAD** cipher (see [TLS](../../transport/tls/)).
Never treat a checksum or CRC as a security control; it only guards against noise.
</Callout>
