## The layer the internet trusts

Every padlock, every `https://`, every API call between services — TLS is the encryption layer
under all of it, and the closest thing the internet has to universal infrastructure for
confidentiality, integrity, and authentication. It wraps *any* byte stream (HTTP, SMTP, IMAP, the
PostgreSQL wire protocol) without understanding it — which is exactly why the OSI model files it at
the **Presentation layer**: it transforms the *representation* of data (to ciphertext) regardless
of meaning, with a session-management aspect (handshake, resumption) bridging into Layer 5.

## What it actually guarantees — and doesn't

Three properties, precisely: **confidentiality** (eavesdroppers see ciphertext), **integrity**
(tampering is detected and aborts the connection), and **server authentication** (you're talking
to who the certificate says — *client* authentication is optional, the basis of mTLS). What it
does **not** hide: that you contacted a given IP, the traffic's size and timing (defeating naive
traffic analysis needs more), and — absent Encrypted Client Hello — the destination hostname in
the SNI. TLS secures the pipe; it says nothing about whether the server itself is trustworthy.

## The 1.3 handshake: one round trip

TLS 1.3 (2018) was a security-driven rewrite that also got dramatically faster:

The decisive moves: the client sends its **key share immediately** (no separate negotiation
round), so keys exist after one exchange; the server's certificate is **encrypted** (1.2 sent it
in clear); and only forward-secret, ephemeral key exchanges survive — static-RSA key transport,
renegotiation, compression, and the weak ciphers behind a decade of attacks (BEAST, CRIME,
POODLE, FREAK) were *deleted from the protocol*, not merely deprecated. **Resumption** uses a
pre-shared key from a prior session; **0-RTT** rides data in the first packet — fast, but
replayable, so it's for idempotent requests only.

## Forward secrecy: the property worth understanding

<Callout variant="insight" title="Why ephemeral keys matter">
TLS 1.3 derives each session's keys from a **fresh, ephemeral** Diffie-Hellman exchange and throws
the private values away afterward. So even if the server's long-term private key is stolen *later*,
yesterday's captured traffic stays unreadable — there's no master key that decrypts the past. This
is **forward secrecy**, and it's why "harvest now, decrypt later" adversaries can't simply wait for
a key compromise. (It's also why the post-quantum migration is urgent for the key *exchange*
specifically — hybrid X25519+ML-KEM is rolling out now.)
</Callout>

## PKI: the trust you're actually leaning on

A certificate is a signed claim — "this public key belongs to example.com" — vouched for by a
**Certificate Authority** your system already trusts (the root store shipped with your OS/browser).
Validation walks a **chain** from the server's cert through intermediates to a trusted root,
checking signatures, validity dates, hostname match, and revocation. The ecosystem's hard-won
improvements: **Certificate Transparency** (every cert logged in public append-only logs, so
mis-issuance is detectable — Chrome requires it), short-lived certs and **ACME/Let's Encrypt**
(automation killed the "expired cert" outage and the excuse not to encrypt), and **OCSP
stapling** (the server proves its cert isn't revoked, sparing a privacy-leaking call to the CA).

<Callout variant="warning" title="The failures you'll actually meet">
Expired certificate (automate renewal — ACME). Hostname mismatch (cert for `www` but not apex, or
vice versa). Incomplete chain — the server forgot to send intermediates, so it works in browsers
that cache them and fails in `curl` (the maddening "works for me" bug). Clock skew making a valid
cert look expired. Mixed TLS versions / no common cipher → handshake failure. `openssl s_client
-connect host:443` shows you the served chain and the negotiated everything — the first tool to
reach for.
</Callout>
